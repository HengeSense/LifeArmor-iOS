Depression

Depression is a medical illness that affects emotions, thoughts, and behavior. Also called major depression, major depressive disorder, and clinical depression, depression is the most common mental disorder. Fortunately, depression is treatable!

Symptoms of Depression

Depression can change or distort the way you see yourself, your life, and those around you. People who have depression usually see everything with a more negative attitude, unable to imagine that any problem or situation can be solved in a positive way. Symptoms of depression can include:
  • Headaches, stomach problems, chronic pains that don't improve with treatment.
  • Thoughts of death or suicide.
  • Memory problems.
  • Poor judgment.
  • Racing thoughts.
  • Always feeling sad, anxious, or "empty".

If you're depressed, you may experience several symptoms at the same time. For example, you may feel tired, have trouble sleeping, and no appetite. Symptoms of depression often last longer than two weeks. Severe symptoms can get in the way of social, work, and family duties. If you think you're depressed, it's best to seek in-person help from a primary care physician or behavioral health professional.

Depression, Thoughts, and Behavior

Moods, thoughts, and behaviors are interconnected. In fact, negative thoughts can lead you to not do activities that might make them feel better. This leads to more negative thinking, resulting in the 'downward spiral' of depression. The 'negative action, negative mood' scenario that typifies depression and which can cause a person to feel "down", tired, sad, or hopeless might also involve the following:

  • Avoiding tasks that require energy or effort.
  • Putting off important tasks until you feel better.
  • Avoiding family or friends.
  • Sleeping a lot or haveing trouble sleeping.
  • Misusing alcohol and other substances.
  • Overeating, or not eating well.
  • Watching TV or playing video games alone.

Automatic Thoughts

"Automatic" thoughts occur without an obvious, "conscious" recognition of their occurrence. Automatic thoughts often reflect what's happening around you or how you feel about yourself. Automatic negative thoughts can contribute to depression. Much of what we think and believe is the result of what we've learned from parents, friends, the media, and society. In fact, some forms of depression are known as learned thinking problems. Since thoughts are "learned," they can be "unlearned" by learning how to think positively.

You can pull yourself out of a downward spiral. The key to finding your way out is to move toward what's important. For example, even if you don't feel motivated, do things that are in line with your values or that set realistic and achievable goals.

Depression and Suicide

The expression of suicidal thoughts should always be taken seriously. While depression carries a high risk of suicide, individuals with suicidal thoughts may not show signs of depression before attempting suicide. Do not hesitate to call your local suicide hotline immediately at 1-800-273-8255. www.suicidepreventionlifeline.org if you or someone you know is experiencing suicidal thoughts. Suicide hotlines are free and confidential and offer support services, treatment, and suicide prevention.

Grief and Depression.

While we all experience grief and loss, each of us is unique in the ways that we cope with our feelings. If at some point your grief encompasses your life and you begin to feel hopeless, helpless, and worthless, it is best to consult with a healthcare professional. Your grieving may have become more reflective of an actual depression. Consider the following:
  • Give yourself permission to grieve - grief is normal and natural.
  • Grieving takes time. Be patient.
  • Spend time with people who have suffered a similar loss. Join a support group and share your story with others.
  • Find helpful people, such as family or friends, a chaplain or other spiritual advisor.
  • If the grief doesn't go away, seek professional help from a psychologist, social worker, or other healthcare professional.
