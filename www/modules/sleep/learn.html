Sleep : Learn

Sleep Basics

  • Our bodies naturally want to be awake during the day and to sleep at night — this natural tendency is referred to as the "circadian rhythm".
  • The circadian rhythm is hard to change, and explains why adjusting to a late-night work schedule can be difficult.
  • Sleep is a complex activity and occurs in five stages, each progressively involving a deeper level of rest.
  • It is during the fifth stage, the "Rapid Eye Movement" or REM stage, that most dreaming occurs.
  • Good sleep is vital to a healthy lifestyle, helping people manage stress and solve problems.
  • Good sleep has been found to accelerate recovery from illness and injury.
  • Lack of sleep can lead to drowsiness, irritability, lack of concentration, and memory and physical problems.

How Much Sleep Do I Need?

While the amount of sleep needed for good health and optimum performance mostly depends on the individual, experts suggest that adults function best when they get seven to nine hours of sleep each night. Ensuring that fatigue isn't a problem during the day is one way of determining how much sleep is needed.

Why Am I Not Sleeping?

From time-to-time, most people encounter occasions during which they are not getting enough sleep. Sleep problems can be caused by stress, pain, drugs, alcohol, diet, lack of exercise, or an irregular sleep schedule, or irregular shift work. People who are not sleeping well are more likely to be depressed, irritable, or anxious. Poor sleep can also lead to problems with attention, concentration, and memory. Chronic sleep problems can hurt relationships, job performance, and increase the risk of accidents.

What is Insomnia?

Insomnia involves one or more of the following symptoms:

  • More than thirty minutes to fall asleep
  • Once asleep, difficulty staying asleep
  • Waking up several times through the night
  • Waking up early then having difficulty getting back to sleep
  • Feeling tired or not well rested
  • Difficulty focusing on tasks
  • Feeling anxious, depressed, or irritable
  • Performing poorly at work due to fatigue

Are Anxiety, Depression, and Insomnia Related?

On the positive side, when confronting potentially dangerous situations, anxiety can prompt alertness and cautiousness. However, while anxiety is a normal reaction to stress, persistent anxiety can lead to sleep problems. Fortunately, as stress diminishes, so does anxiety.

Sleep problems can be caused by a depressed mood. Depression can cause changes in body temperature. Individuals with depression often sleep lightly and feel less rested when they wake up. There can also be changes in the amount of dreaming that they do.

Where depression or anxiety is causing or worsening a sleep problem, treating the mood problem often helps with the sleep problem, too. In turn, sleeping better helps improve mood problems. A healthcare professional can help you determine if you're having a mood problem. You can also practice relaxation techniques and healthy sleep habits suggested on afterdeployment.org.

What is Sleep Apnea?

Sleep apnea is a condition during which a person stops breathing for several seconds to minutes. The throat briefly closes, blocking air from the lungs. People with sleep apnea may wake up often during the night gasping for breath. Sleep interruptions can cause exhaustion and irritability. In severe cases, sleep apnea can be deadly.

You may have sleep apnea, if you:

  • Snore loudly
  • Are overweight
  • Have high blood pressure
  • Have small airways in your nose, throat, or mouth
  • Have a family history of sleep apnea

If you have sleep apnea, avoiding alcohol, tobacco, and drugs, losing weight, and sleeping on your side (instead of on your back) may be helpful. However, sleep apnea is a serious health problem and should be evaluated by a health care provider.

What is Narcolepsy?

Narcolepsy is a medical disorder. Individuals with narcolepsy have trouble staying awake or fall asleep suddenly and without warning. This condition can occur while driving, working, eating, and even during dangerous activities. While some people have specific triggers that bring on the condition, the triggers can be difficult to identify.

Risk factors include certain thyroid disorders, diabetes, a family history of narcolepsy, and having an autoimmune disorder.

Although there is no cure, narcolepsy requires medical intervention. Lifestyle changes can also help control the problem, including routine compliance with prescription medications, taking naps, and practicing good sleep hygiene like the tips taught on afterdeployment.org.

What is Restless Leg Syndrome?

People with Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS) have crawling, tingling, or burning sensations in their legs or arms. RLS sensations make it difficult to fall asleep or stay asleep. People with RLS may also suffer from Periodic Limb Movement disorder which causes a jerking movement of the legs and arms. These movements happen roughly every 20-40 seconds, waking the person.

If you think you have RLS, consult with a health care provider. You can also make some lifestyle changes, such as avoiding alcohol, tobacco, caffeine, and certain medications that make the condition worse, exercising regularly, massaging your arms and legs before bed, using heat or ice packs on your arms and legs, and practicing good sleep hygiene, like the tips taught on afterdeployment.org.

Is My Sleep Problem All In My Head?

Sleep problems can have many causes, from genetics to diet to stress. While no one has control over all of the possible causes, it is important to work on the things that can be controlled. One of those areas is learning to control negative thoughts about sleeping.

Because your body reacts to frustration, negative thoughts at bedtime can set off a negative cycle that leads to insomnia. Your heart may race, your muscles may tense, and your head may start to ache. Your discomfort makes you worry, which makes you restless. Falling asleep becomes harder and may feel impossible.

For tricks on how to reconsider your negative thoughts, check out afterdeployment.org.

Why Am I Having Nightmares?

Occasional nightmares are normal. Usually, nightmares tend to be about stressful or traumatic events. Service members often find they have more nightmares after a stressful or traumatic deployment. Traumatic means a life-threatening or terrifying event that produces intense fear, helplessness, or horror. These types of reactions are often associated with Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder.

To cope with nightmares and sleep terrors, some people use alcohol or drugs, or even avoid sleeping. Unfortunately, these methods not only worsen the sleep problem, they often lead to new problems. There are many suggestions for managing nightmares on afterdeployment.org.

Are Nightmares and Sleep Terrors the Same Thing?

Nightmares and sleep terrors are not the same thing. During a nightmare, the traumatic event is "relived." The same fear, rage, or helplessness that was felt during the actual event may be re-experienced.

Most people don't remember sleep terrors when they wake up. Any sleep terror memories are single images — not a story like in a nightmare. Sleep terrors can be so frightening or terrifying that they produce screaming, shaking, sweating, a racing heart, confusion, and difficulty calming down. When the sleep terror ends, the individual can calm down and return to normal sleep.

How Can I Get Regular/Good Sleep?

Sleep behavior and sleep habits have a large impact on the ability to achieve regular and restful sleep. Sleep habits can be harmful but also healthy. At night, the body and the mind should be prepared for sleep. Here are some things to do at least one hour before bedtime to help with relaxing and avoiding harmful sleep habits.

  • Listen to soft music or engage in light reading.
  • Take a warm bath or shower.
  • Avoid things that are physically or mentally demanding, like working, studying, playing video games, or watching violent movies or television shows.
  • Keep your bedroom peaceful and comfortable.
  • Dim the lights and keep it quiet; darkness and quiet tell the body it's time to sleep.
  • Keep a comfortable bedroom temperature (not too cold, not too hot).
  • Follow the same relaxing sleep ritual every night.

If I Can't Fall Asleep, Shouldn't I Just Stay In Bed Until I Do?

It's not good to stay in bed if you can't fall or stay asleep. Lying awake in bed teaches the body that the bed is a place to stay awake, rather than a place to sleep. If you can't sleep, don't stay in bed longer than thirty minutes. Go to another room and do something quiet and relaxing. Return to bed when you feel drowsy.

Should I Try To Catch Up When I've Lost Sleep?

Unfortunately, it's impossible to "catch up" on lost sleep. Sleeping in late or going to bed early may cause your body to learn a new, harmful sleep pattern. Don't allow yourself to stay in bed for more than an hour longer than you would get with a normal night's sleep. If you want to overcome sleep problems, set a sleep schedule and stick to it.

What About Nicotine? Can Smoking Or Chewing Impact Sleep?

Because nicotine is a stimulant, it can actually interfere with sleep. Don't smoke, chew or dip tobacco near bedtime.

Won't Alcohol Help Me To Sleep?

It's best to avoid alcohol at night. While alcohol can make falling asleep easier, this effect only lasts a few hours. As alcohol starts to wear off, it's actually harder to stay asleep or sleep restfully.

Is There Anything I Should Be Doing When I Wake In The Mornings?

Morning is the time for both the body and the mind to wake up. In the morning, light filtering in to the bedroom should be increased. Bright light tells the brain it's time to wake up! Research shows that it's best to wake up at the same time every day, even on weekends.
