Military Sexual Trauma : Learn

What Is Sexual Harassment?

Sexual harassment is unwelcome verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature that occurs in the workplace or an academic or training setting. Sexual harassment includes gender harassment (e.g., put you down because of your gender), unwanted sexual attention (e.g., made offensive remarks about your sexual activities or your body) and sexual coercion (e.g., implied special treatment if you were sexually cooperative).

What Is Sexual Assault?

Sexual assault is any sort of sexual activity between at least two people in which one of the people is involved against his or her will. Physical force may or may not be used. The sexual activity involved can include many different experiences including unwanted touching, grabbing, oral sex, anal sex, sexual penetration with an object, and/or sexual intercourse.

The Impact Of Sexual Assault and Harassment Include:

There are a wide range of emotional reactions in response to Military Sexual Trauma. Even in the aftermath of severe forms of victimization, there is no one way that victims will respond. Instead, the intensity, duration, and trajectory of psychological responses will all vary based on previous trauma history, their appraisal of the traumatic event, and the quality of their support systems following the trauma.

Sexual assault victimization is associated with high lifetime rates of post-traumatic stress in both men and women. Sexual Harassment is associated with poorer psychological well-being, more physical problems and lower satisfaction with health and work. Major depressive disorder (MDD) is another common reaction following sexual assault, with research suggesting that almost a third of sexual assault victims have at least one period of MDD during their lives. Victims of sexual assault may also report increased substance use, perhaps as a means of managing other psychological symptoms. In addition to these psychological conditions, victims of sexual trauma may continue to struggle with a range of other symptoms that interfere with their quality of life. Common emotional reactions include anger and shame, guilt or self-blame. Victims of sexual trauma may report problems in their interpersonal relationships, including difficulties with trust, difficulties engaging in social activities or sexual dysfunction. Male victims of sexual trauma may also express concern about their sexuality or their masculinity.

If You Are Sexually Assaulted:

  • Get to a safe place.
  • Call 911-if you need immediate medical help.
  • Call Military OneSource (1.800.345.9647) anytime to talk with a Sexual Assault Response Coordinator (SARC) or Victim Advocate (VA).
  • Get medical care as soon as possible. While you may not have visible physical injuries, you may be at risk for a sexually transmitted disease or pregnancy.
    • Ask healthcare personnel to conduct a Sexual Assault Forensic Examination (SAFE) to preserve evidence of the assault.
    • If you think you've been drugged, ask for your urine to be tested and provide a sample.
    • Save all evidence of the assault. Avoid bathing, washing your hands or brushing your teeth before seeking medical care. Do not clean or straighten up the scene.
  • Write down or record any details you remember about the assault and your assailant.

If You Are Sexually Harassed

  • Stay away from your harasser and make sure you're not left alone with the person.
  • Call Military OneSource (1.800.345.9647) anytime for confidential support or advice.
  • Consider talking to a chaplain or a mental health counselor with confidentiality.
  • Record any contact you have with your harasser. Include dates, times and what was said and done each time.

Assault By Known Individuals

Most victims are not raped by strangers. Up to 85% of rapists are known by the person they attack. "Acquaintance rape" by a friend or co-worker is common, especially among young single women. Studies find 50% of sexual assaults occur in or around a woman's home, and 50% happen during the day in civilian life. In the military, sexual assaults generally happen between Thursday and Sunday from the hours of 2200 to 0600.

Drinking and Sexual Assault

Sexual assault survivors are never responsible for their assailant's behavior, no matter how much alcohol they've had. Although alcohol may increase the risk of sexual assault it can also leave people unable to consent or protect themselves. Drinking is not the cause of the assault.

How Serious is Sexual Harassment?

Sexual Harassment is not harmless flirtation and can be devastating to a victim. Victims often feel offended, frightened and insulted. Research shows victims often must leave their schools or jobs in order to avoid harassment. Many victims suffer serious psychological and health-related problems, including post-traumatic stress (PTS), depression, and anxiety.

How Common Is Sexual Violence In The Military

It's difficult to give precise data on how common sexual assault and harassment are, because many service members who are assaulted or harassed do not report what happened for fear of being ignored, stigmatized, or retaliated against. Due to the fear of these retributions as few as 5-20% of sexual assaults are reported to an authority figure by a service member.

Statistics show that 23-28% of female service members are sexually assaulted while in the military and 11% are raped. A report published in 2006 found that 6.8% of active duty women were sexually assaulted in a single year. Statistics of sexual harassment of women vary, ranging from 34%-78% of military women.

While male sexual assault and harassment are vastly underreported in the military, one estimate claims 3.5% of male service members are sexually assaulted while in the military, and 1.2% are raped. A 2006 report found 1.8% of active duty men were sexually assaulted in a single year. Statistics of sexual harassment vary, ranging from 6-38% of men in the military.

Reporting rates continue to rise among active duty service members serving in Iraq and Afghanistan. Historically, there are higher rates of assault during a war. The greater number of incidents may be due to the changes in policy in 2005 encouraging survivors to report. In the 2005 policy change, survivors can report and incident without opening an investigation.

Facts About Military Sexual Trauma (MST) In The Military

  • The most common MST is acquaintance or date rape, also known as 'offender-known rape'.
  • Alcohol and unlocked barracks doors are common factors in MSTs of service members.
  • Sexual assaults generally occur between Thursday and Sunday, and between the hours or 2200 - 0600.
  • The majority of MSTs took place at a military work site, during duty hours by military personnel.
  • The Service branch with the highest percentage of women reporting sexual trauma is the Marine Corps.
  • Victims and perpetrators tend to be military personnel ranks E1-E4, ages 17-26 years old, and unmarried.

What Happens When An Assault Is Reported In The Military?

The common response to a reported incident is an administrative action, a letter of reprimand or lost pay. In rare cases, offenders are court-martialed.

Of those who received disciplinary action by command, only 25% were court-martialed. About 5% of all investigations led to a court martial, 8% to non-judicial punishment and 7% to administrative disciplinary action or discharge instead of other punishment.

How Is Military Sexual Trauma (MST) Different From Combat Trauma?

Service members often expect to face trauma in the line of duty; however, sexual assault or harassment is different. MST survivors who are a victim of someone within their own ranks often feel trapped as they live and work with, or rely on their perpetrators daily. Survivors may also face a disruption in their career goals. The attacker may have power or influence over the survivor's career advancement. Or survivors may choose to leave the military to escape their attackers. In the war zone, victims may rely on their attackers for medical and mental health care. This reliance on perpetrators can leave survivors feeling helpless, powerless, betrayed, angry, and alone. If you must rely on your attacker for any reason, you may feel disoriented or confused. MST survivors may also be at an increased risk of becoming a victim again.

Worrying About Compromising The Mission

If loyalty, teamwork, and mission success are highly valued, victims may hesitate to report the incident because they worry about the impact on the unit and mission. Survivors may also worry about their own reputations, appearing vulnerable in a military culture or others placing blame on them for what happened. MST survivors in the military say they were not believed, or were encouraged to stay quiet. Being "hushed up" can hinder their recovery and leave survivors feeling ignored and alone. The Department of Defense and the Armed Services are working to ensure that survivors are heard, believed, and treated with respect and understanding.

Post-traumatic Stress Disorder (PTS) and Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTS) includes:

  • Having unwanted, distressing memories or nightmares of the trauma.
  • Avoiding reminders of the trauma, feeling emotionally numb and disconnected from others.
  • Struggling with chronically high levels of tension and alertness.

Rates of lifetime post-traumatic stress are high among men and women who have been sexually assaulted. Research indicates that veterans who experience sexual trauma in the military develop post-traumatic stress at higher rates than those exposed to combat trauma, with 28.1% of female Veterans Affairs (VA) patients who reported a military sexual trauma and 18.4% of male patients having post-traumatic stress. This is due to the ongoing stress of living and working with the attacker, often in the same environment where they were attacked. Up to 60% of women who survived a military sexual trauma reported post-traumatic stress in one study. Research also shows that 65% of men who are raped develop post-traumatic stress.

Depression And Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Sexual trauma survivors often say they feel sad, "blue," or depressed. Many suffer loss of pleasure or joy, low energy and motivation, hopelessness and low self-esteem. It's normal to feel sad and upset after being assaulted or harassed. Depression interferes with the lives of many sexual trauma survivors, forcing them to seek professional treatment. One study of military sexual trauma survivors found nearly 46% of female and more than 28% of male survivors suffered from depression.

Anxiety And Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Survivors may suffer from anxiety attacks. These attacks may come "out of the blue" or when the victim is reminded of the experience. Victims may constantly worry about possible dangers or difficulties and "brace" or prepare for and protect themselves from future attacks. It's important to know these are natural reactions for survivors to have when their sense of safety has been violated. If high anxiety is persistent you may want to consider seeking behavioral health care.

Emotional Numbness and Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Many survivors of sexual trauma alternate between intense feelings and no feelings at all. Difficulty having feelings is also known as emotional numbness. Emotional numbness may mean you feel emotionally 'flat', or have difficulty experiencing happiness or love. In some instances survivors say they no longer know who they are, or what matters to them.

Problems With Alcohol Or Other Drugs After Military Sexual Trauma

Survivors often drink alcohol, use illegal drugs, or overuse prescription drugs to cope with intense feelings or sleep problems. One study found rape survivors were 3.4 times more likely to use marijuana, 6 times more likely to use cocaine, and 10 times more likely to use other major drugs than people who had not been raped. Using drugs and alcohol to sleep or escape often backfires. Remember, sleep quality suffers when abusing alcohol or drugs and your "medicated" emotions and memories resurface when you sober up.

Sleep Problems And Military Sexual Trauma

The brains and bodies of survivors stay on alert and ready for danger after a traumatic experience and it's common to have trouble falling or staying asleep. Often survivors may avoid sleeping if they are experiencing disturbing nightmares or terrors that interrupt their sleep regularly.

Difficulties With Attention, Concentration, And Memory After a Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Survivors may struggle with concentration, attention and memory, which may affect their work performance and strain relationships. Some survivors may not remember the event, or have vague or disjointed memories. Some may have "checked out" (present in body but not in spirit) during the experience. They may now feel detached or disconnected from their bodies or experience.

Shame And Self-Blame After Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Survivors often feel shame or guilt over their trauma due to common misconceptions about sexual assault and harassment. Victims may blame themselves for what happened or regret decisions made before the trauma. They may feel humiliated or embarrassed over what they were forced or coerced to do. Victims place blame on themselves as a way to regain control over their lives. Remember, you should never take responsibility for your attacker's choices and behaviors.

Suicidal Thoughts After Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Survivors may think about or attempt suicide to escape the fear, anger, depression and other intense feelings brought on after the MST. We strongly suggest that you reach out to others if you are thinking about suicide. If you need immediate crisis counseling, call the Veterans Crisis Line at 1-800-273-TALK. Counselors are available 24/7.

Difficulties In Relationships After Military Sexual Trauma

Relationships with loved ones, especially intimate relationships, can be deeply affected when one partner suffers a sexual trauma. Survivors may feel isolated or disconnected, and may have difficulty trusting others. This can lead to distance, fear, and difficulty with intimacy in relationships. Some survivors experience the double trauma of coming home to a violent or abusive relationship.

Difficulties At Work After Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Some sexual trauma survivors have an increased need for control after feeling powerless during the attack. This can create struggles with Commanding Officers (or employers) over issues of authority, management and fairness in the workplace. Survivors have reported lower work satisfaction, especially in circumstances where the victim continues to work where the trauma took place and with their attacker(s). In some situations victims quit their jobs (including military careers) when they were unable to manage interactions with their attacker on a day-to-day basis, or when their work environments did not have an adequate support system in place.

Sexuality After Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Sexual trauma affects one's sexuality and sex life. Medical issues may arise in the form of an STD or an unwanted pregnancy. Survivors may also see changes in their sexual responses (e.g., lack of desire, lack of orgasm, or painful intercourse). Sexual experiences may be interrupted by anxiety, fear, angry reactions, or unexpected memories of the incident. Intimacy may feel vulnerable and uncomfortable leading survivors to use alcohol or drugs to escape their sexual feelings. Some survivors practice risky sex or become highly sexual to regain a sense of control. Others may "shut down" physically and emotionally during sex after an assault. If you've been sexually assaulted or harassed, it's important for you to focus on safety and comfort in your sexual experiences. Some survivors experience involuntary sexual arousal in response to sexual trauma. This involuntary response leads some survivors to wonder if they enjoyed the violation or are "perverted." Some may question their sexuality or sexual orientation after the attack.

Physical Health Problems After Military Sexual Assault

During a sexual assault sexually transmitted diseases (STD's) or an unwanted pregnancy may be acquired. Sexual trauma is also linked to reproductive health problems, chronic back pain, pelvic pain, headaches, eating disorders, gastrointestinal problems, weight problems, and chronic fatigue. Survivors of MST are also more likely to drink and smoke and suffer from additional health problems (liver and lung disease). While female survivors suffer from weight and thyroid problems, male survivors are more susceptible to contracting HIV/AIDS.

Spiritual Crisis Following Sexual Trauma

Sexual trauma often leads to a spiritual crisis, especially for survivors who were spiritual or religious before they were assaulted or harassed. It is not rare for some survivors to think their trauma is a form of spiritual punishment. While some survivors gain a deeper faith due to their hardship, some become angry at God or life itself and lose their faith completely.

Men And Sexual Assault Or Harassment

While sexual assault is more common among women, men are also victimized by rape, harassment, and other forms of sexual assault. Either women or men can be the perpetrator. In the military, 23-28% of women, and 3.5% of men report being sexually assaulted. However, about the same number of people from each gender experience sexual assault, because men greatly outnumber women in the military.

Male and female survivors react in similar ways to sexual trauma. However, men are often less willing to seek support. Many sexual assaults of men involve more than one attacker, weapons, or forced participation. Male survivors admit to feeling isolated, alienated from others, emotionally vulnerable, and insecure about their masculinity.

Sexual assault for a man can lead to shame and self-doubt. It may also trigger negative self-judgments and cause male victims to question their masculinity. Men may feel confused if their bodies were sexually aroused involuntarily during the attack. This is often out of the victims control and in many cases the responses are muscular or involuntary. It's important to inform the victim that these reactions are not measures of pleasure or interest. Alternatively, in some cases, the assault may be somewhat pleasurable physically - but that does not mean the victim asked for, wanted, or consented to it.

If you're a male survivor, you are not alone. Seeking help from loved ones or professionals can help you overcome your shame and isolation. Seeking help takes courage and strength, the courage to face what happened, and strength to admit and overcome your vulnerabilities. But with commitment, discipline, and the right support, you can recover from sexual trauma.

Women As Survivors Of Military Sexual Trauma (MST)

Women survivors of military sexual trauma face unique challenges as well. Rates of sexual assault and harassment are higher among service members than among civilians. This can potentially be linked to the reality that women are the minority in all the Services and in positions where they can be targets of violence.

Female service member survivors may not be comfortable in the more traditional role of seeking help. They fear some may think they are just causing trouble or undermining the group's strength. Female survivors may also worry that speaking up will damage unity and morale, especially if their attacker is a co-worker or fellow service member. And because many women find it hard break into the military "boys club," some worry coming forward will interfere with social and career opportunities or they won't be believed or taken seriously. However, by remaining silent, they are isolating themselves and may have a difficult time healing.

Who To Report Sexual Violation To

The United States Military has developed a system to provide support and resources to service members who have been sexually violated.

To help military sexual trauma survivors, each branch has Sexual Assault Response Coordinators (SARC) to coordinate sexual assault victim care. SARCs are available 24/7 every day of the year. You don't need an appointment or referral to contact a SARC. If you have the number, call your local SARC. Or call Military OneSource to be connected to a SARC.

If you want, the SARC will also assign you a Victim Advocate (VA). Victim Advocates provide support, liaison services and care. They provide crisis intervention, and they assist victims in receiving necessary services, referrals, and on-going non-clinical support. The VA provides information on available options and resources so you can make informed decisions about medical and mental health care and about reporting the assault. At your request, your VA can also go with you to investigative interviews, medical examinations and follow-up appointments. However, your VA does not make decisions or speak for you and won't interfere with the medical, investigative, or judicial processes.

SARC's make sure you and other sexual assault victims get appropriate and responsive care and can help you determine if you should report your incident. They'll address any concerns you have about your health or well-being. And if you decide to report, the SARC will help you from the initial report through disposition of any legal case. Your SARC coordinator may ask some general questions about the incident to make sure you get medical and emotional help. All communication with the SARC is confidential and visits to the SARC will not affect your commissioning or PQ status.

The Difference Between Unrestricted And Restricted Reporting

The military offers victims two different options for reporting an assault: Restricted and Unrestricted. These options address privacy concerns while making sure you receive medical care and counseling.

  • Unrestricted Reporting

    Unrestricted Reporting is a complete report of a sexual assault to military and legal authorities that activates victims' services, including medical treatment and counseling. Unrestricted Reporting also notifies chain of command and triggers an official investigation. The Unrestricted Reporting process can be started by chain of command, law enforcement, a SARC, or even healthcare providers. When they receive a report of sexual assault, the SARC will immediately assign a Victims Advocate (VA). At the victim's request, a healthcare provider will conduct a Sexual Assault Forensic Examination (SAFE), which may involve collecting evidence. Access to details about the incident will be given only to those personnel with a legitimate need to know, such as the VA, chain of command, medical personnel, and law enforcement. Currently, military retirees, dependents, and other civilian victims may only use Unrestricted Reporting. In unrestricted reporting, victims are also assigned a Victim Witness Liaison (VWL). The VWL helps victims through the legal investigation process. The VWL will explain the victim's rights and keep the victim updated on the legal proceedings for the case. The VA works with the VWL to provide victims with information about where to get emergency medical care, social service support, and mental health treatment.

  • Restricted Reporting

    Restricted Reporting was created in 2005 to allow victims to confidentially disclose details of their assault without triggering an official investigation or notifying chain of command. Like the Unrestricted option, Restricted Reporting includes confidential medical treatment and counseling for the victim.

    If they choose the Restricted Reporting option, service members must report the assault to a SARC, a VA, a healthcare provider, or a chaplain. Generally, the assigned SARC, Victims Advocate (VA), and healthcare providers will not disclose confidential communications from a Restricted Report. But the SARC must report general information about the incident to command within 24 hours. If the victim reports the sexual assault to a healthcare provider, the provider will offer appropriate treatment, and report the assault to the SARC instead of law enforcement or command. When they receive a report of sexual assault, the SARC will assign a VA to the victim. At the victim's request, a healthcare provider will conduct a Sexual Assault Forensic Examination (SAFE), which may involve collecting evidence.

    Restricted reporting does have disadvantages. You cannot be protected from your attacker if the military is unaware you need protection. Only health care professionals, chaplains, SARCs, and VAs have a duty to maintain confidentiality. Others, including friends, may have to report the assault.

*NOTE: Making a Restricted report does not prevent victims from later filing an Unrestricted report for the same incident.

Delayed Reports Of Sexual Assault

Reporting a sexual trauma immediately after the incident helps ensure all possible evidence is collected and preserved - in case you choose to pursue legal action. Timely reporting also provides the best chance to gather accurate testimony if there were any witnesses. But victims can report a sexual assault at any time and the investigation will be the same, no matter how much time has passed. Late reporting can impact other investigations involving the same attacker, and may help identify and prosecute a criminal. If you aren't sure whether to report an assault, you should discuss your concerns about reporting with a professional counselor. You can also speak to the Office of Special Investigations (OSI) or a legal representative to ask about investigative or legal processes.

Collateral Misconduct

Some victims worry about reporting sexual assault because they were involved in illegal conduct at the time of the assault, such as underage drinking or fraternization. This conduct may be considered collateral misconduct by the military. Collateral misconduct means that unit commanders have the option to delay disciplinary action for the misconduct until after the sexual assault case is resolved, although deferring discipline is not required. When choosing disciplinary actions for a victim's collateral misconduct, commanders and supervisors consider the impact on the victim. They will not choose disciplinary action that further traumatizes the victim or discourages others from reporting sexual assault. If you file a Restricted Report, beware that disciplinary action will not be deferred.

Legal Investigation Process

Investigations can be stressful for victims of sexual assault. The process from investigation to courts martial or other punishment can take months or years. During an investigation, victims may be asked difficult and personal questions. At least once a month, command should update victims on the investigation, until final disposition of the case. "Final disposition" means the conclusion of any judicial, non-judicial and administrative actions (including separation actions and "no action" decisions).

Work Transfers And Administrative Separation

Military sexual trauma survivors may choose to apply for work transfers or separate from the service as a result of their trauma. Another option to explore is "no contact orders." Service members who live off post may also file for restraining orders from civilian courts. These decisions can be difficult, and some may interpret a transfer or separation as punishing the victim for reporting. Considering a transfer or separation may be the only way for a victim to stay safe and emotionally healthy.

Stages Of Recovery For Emotional Healing

Everyone reacts differently to sexual assault and harassment. The following are general stages of recovery that nearly all victims experience. These stages do not need to go in order. Recovery is not a linear process and survivors may pass through these stages at different times.

  • Initial shock. Immediately after the trauma, survivors may experience emotional and physical shock. This can be seen in the form of crying, screaming, shaking, or becoming very quiet and withdrawn.
  • Denial. When in denial, survivors attempt to go back to their normal routine. They avoid thinking about the assault, or they deny its impact.
  • Reactivation. During this stage survivors experience a reoccurrence of intense feelings (i.e., depression, anxiety, etc.) linked to the sexual trauma and will often reach out for help or counseling.
  • Healing. Survivors begin to process their anger, grief, fear, and emotional turmoil in the healing stage. Survivors address the anger they feel toward themselves, friends, significant others, society, or the legal system. They are able to process their grief over the change that has occurred in their life since the trauma (i.e., loss of relationships, betrayed trust or lost innocence). It is in this stage that survivors begin to reclaim their lives.
  • Integration. With support, hard work, and time, many survivors integrate the trauma into their larger life story. Survivors may view life with different priorities and their recovery can be a source of insight, political activism, and personal growth.

Final Tips To Work Toward A Positive Recovery

  • Create Safety: Secure a safe place to live, work and sleep..
  • Self-Care: Manage any health concerns and seek support or counseling if you feel like hurting yourself or someone else. Avoid dangerous behaviors like alcohol abuse, drug use, unsafe sex and reckless driving.
  • Get Support: Survivors tend to isolate themselves. Those who seek support from family, friends or a professional recover better than those who try to "go it alone."
  • Set Boundaries: Be firm, gracious and direct. When faced with resistance, repeat your statement of request.
  • Develop Coping Skills: Everyone has different ways of coping with stress and upsetting events. Use relaxation tools, conflict resolution, and problem-solving to manage these life stresses as they arise.
  • Revisit the Experience and its Effects: This process includes acknowledging the impact of the sexual violation, and expressing some of the feelings associated with it. For some, therapies like Prolonged Exposure Therapy and Cognitive Processing Therapy can ease intense feelings and fear and allow survivors to rethink their beliefs about the trauma.
  • Reach Out and Help Others: Some survivors can reclaim a feeling of healthy power by advocating for themselves or other survivors or by doing something to support those in need.

Unhealthy Coping Methods

  • Emotional numbing: Can include avoiding feelings of anger, fear, happiness and love in order to avoid the emotional pain from the trauma.
  • Dissociation: Is a way the body copes with an unbearable experience. Dissociation, meaning "spacing out," "checking out," or even losing memory for periods of time can become a habit-forming way to deal with stress or intense feelings.
  • Obsessive-Compulsive behavior: Some survivors engage in obsessive unwanted and repeated thoughts, feelings, ideas, sensations (obsessions), or behaviors that make them feel driven to do something (compulsions). Often the person carries out the behaviors to get rid of the obsessive thoughts, but this only provides temporary relief. Instead, these obsessions and compulsions end up controlling the survivor.
  • Alcohol/substance abuse: Some survivors turn to alcohol and drugs to escape from upsetting memories and emotions. However, these substances can hinder the recover process by creating health and relationship problems.
  • Eating disorders: Survivors may try to gain control by binge-eating, purging (bulimia nervosa), or not eating at all (anorexia nervosa). Eating disorders can cause heart and kidney problems and even death. Getting help from a behavioral health counselor or nutritionist early is important.

Preventing Assault and Revictimization

Most sexual victimizations are committed by acquaintances. The following are general suggestions for reducing your risk of sexual assault.

  • Be assertive and communicate your limits clearly. For example, be direct about reaching an understanding with a friend or date. Sex without consent is rape, whether the coercion used is physical or emotional.
  • Remember that alcohol and drugs impair not only your judgment but also your ability to make that judgment clear to someone else. If you are socializing with a group or friends that are using alcohol or drugs, their judgment, and the ability to understand you, will also be impaired.
  • Be prepared. Consider enrolling in a self defense class where you will learn techniques on how to maintain awareness of your surroundings, how to use objects to defend yourself and how to attack the most vulnerable points of a body.

When Should I Consider Seeking Help From a Behavioral Health Counselor?

After a military sexual trauma you may experience strong feelings that interfere with your ability to function. Consider seeking outside help if you experience:

  • Alcohol or substance abuse
  • Extremely vivid memories of the event, like it's happening again
  • Suicidal or Homicidal thoughts
  • Self-destructive behavior (i.e., cutting or other self-mutilation)
  • Feeling disoriented or out of touch with reality
  • Increased symptoms of a pre-existing condition
  • Hallucinations
  • Hyperventilation
  • Uncontrollable shaking
  • No memories of periods of time
