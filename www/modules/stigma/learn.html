What Is Stigma?

Stigma is rejection, discrimination, or any other mode of separating people based on their characteristics. An individual or group is stigmatized if they are seen as different or "worse-off" than their peers. Individuals can stigmatize themselves as well. For example, if you believe that you are "weak" and "incompetent" because you can't solve a problem like depression or PTSD on your own, you are self-labeling and could stigmatize yourself.

Stigma has four parts:
  1. Labeling someone with a condition (e.g., saying someone is "depressed").
  2. Stereotyping people with that condition (e.g., thinking that every depressed person is "weak").
  3. Creating a division between "us" and a devalued "them" group (e.g., thinking "unlike us, depressed people make lousy soldiers").
  4. Discriminating against someone on the basis of their label (e.g., excluding a depressed colleague from after duty activities).

Military Myths and Stigma

There are many myths about mental and physical injuries and the treatment process for these problems. These myths can create stigma. You may have heard any of these myths expressed as facts by commanding officers, other service members, family, friends, or even by yourself. These myths are not facts. They are labels and they can lead to stigma. Understanding that these "facts" are really myths with no basis in truth can help you avoid labeling, and stigmatizing, yourself and others. Some common myths are:
  • "Everyone who goes to war gets PTSD."
  • "Commanders and respected service members don't struggle with post-deployment issues."
  • "Real warriors can harden themselves to perform with little to no sleep."
  • "People who seek professional help dishonor their families."
  • "Anyone who has seen a mental health professional is unfit for duty."
  • "Anything you say in therapy or to your chaplain can be shared with your command."
  • "Courage in combat requires the absence of fear."
  • "People with brain injuries never get better."
  • "Only women get sexually assaulted."
  • "Thinking about suicide or having thoughts of death means a person will commit suicide."

Fear of Stigma

Service members and their families can fear being stigmatized. This fear keeps many service members from talking to friends and family or seeking treatment. For families, the fear of stigma comes in two ways. Families may resist seeking help for problems at home because they don't want to cast doubt on the service member's fitness for duty. Family members may also fear stigmatizing themselves by admitting their family needs help, e.g., "my husband needs counseling because I'm not a supportive wife."

Fear of stigma can cause families to keep post-deployment issues secret from everyone, including each other. Maintaining the "secret" of their returning service member's difficulties can damage relationships within the family, among friends, and even at work. Instead of asking for help, service members with post-deployment challenges may give up on personal goals that now seem out of reach. Friends, family, colleagues, and even service members themselves can become disappointed and angry with the changes. Even with the best efforts to mask problems, work can suffer and struggling service members can be "found out" by command or colleagues.

When fear of stigma interferes with seeking help, it can cause serious problems. Studies show that recent service members who have returned from deployment are at an increased risk for suicide. Many deaths could be avoided if service members felt comfortable asking for help.

Using Realistic Thinking to Get Over Stigma

In a combat environment, you were taught to think through worst-case-scenarios. In unpredictable and dangerous situations this is a critical survival skill. In the non-combat environment, thinking of worst-case scenarios actually makes more trouble than it prevents. Remember, the worst case is only one possible outcome. You can choose to focus on the probable instead of the possible and avoid trapping yourself in believing that the worst case is the most likely case.

Realistic thinking takes into consideration what is probably going to happen instead of focusing on the best- or worst-case scenario. Realistic thinking has two parts when it comes to receiving help:
  1. Think about whether you have changed in a way that is causing problems for you or the people around you. Deployment changes everyone in positive and negative ways because it is such an intense and unique experience. Many of the effects of deployment will reduce naturally over time. However, if you are having trouble readjusting, or if the people around you notice negative changes in you, try identifying exactly what has changed and how much of a problem the change is causing.
  2. Think through what will probably happen if you go for help -- not what might possibly happen. Make a list of pros and cons to help you identify the probable and possible outcomes for getting help. Notice there are many more likely possibilities than the worst-case scenario.

Overcome Stigma by Getting Input from Someone You Know and Respect

One of the best ways to overcome stigma is to get input from someone you know and respect who has received counseling. You can ask about their experiences, how they decided to get help, and any positive or negative effects that had on their home and work. If you don't know anyone who has gone for counseling, challenge yourself to speak with at least one person you trust. They may be able to assist with weighing out the pros and cons of seeking professional care.

Here are some tips for talking with others about post-deployment challenges:
  • Be prepared. Know what you want to say and how you are likely to feel so you will not be caught off-guard and end up saying more or less than you intended.
  • Tell them what you need from them. Do you want them to just listen, help you make a decision about seeking help, provide another viewpoint, or let you know if your view of a situation is right?
  • Test the waters. Say a little and see how they do with it. This will also give you the opportunity to see how you do with the telling.

Help Others to Combat Stigma

If people know someone they respect who has sought help, they are more likely to not feel stigma about their post-deployment issues and getting help if needed. So, if you have seen a counselor or health care professional, take a risk and let other people know. You may feel uncertain about this, but your bravery can help others to make challenging decisions about their health and well-being.

Even if you have never received treatment, you can be open and supportive in giving your fellow service members an opportunity to talk. Be a comrade: look for signs that others are struggling and do what you can to help them overcome their post-deployment challenges. Stigma can be isolating, so don't assume that someone else has already told them to seek help. You may be the only person who actually makes the effort to support them.

Are There Consequences for Seeking Help in the Military?

While many service members worry that their security clearance will be revoked if they see a behavioral health professional, the truth is that less than one percent of those investigated for clearances are rejected solely on the basis of their mental health profiles. In fact, in May 2008, Defense Secretary Robert M. Gates announced that the Department of Defense had revised "the infamous question 21" on its security clearance questionnaire. Now, people who seek help for combat-related issues or who receive marital counseling don't have to answer "yes" when asked if they have ever "consulted with a health care professional regarding an emotional or mental health condition."

Being aware of stigma and rejecting myths about treatment helps you be in control of the decision to seek help. A recent study showed that of the service members who self-referred for mental health treatment, only three percent had a negative career impact. In comparison, 39 percent of service members who were referred by their commanders had a negative career impact. Colonel Scott Marrs, Ph.D., Chief of the Air Force's mental health division said: "Seeking mental health care doesn't harm your career. It's not being able to do your job because of personal issues that can harm your career."

Getting Help for Deployment-Related Problems

Help is available to you. You have bravely served and sacrificed for your country and it is your right to receive care and support. It is in your best interest, and the interest of your family, friends, and mission for you to get the help you need. A safe and effective force is strong and mentally healthy. There is courage in asking for help, strength in learning to manage struggles, and pride in moving on from challenges.
