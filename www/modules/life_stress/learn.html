What Is Stress?

Stress is a normal physical response to events that make you feel threatened or upset your balance in some way. When you sense danger, whether it's real or imagined, the body's defenses kick into a high gear in a rapid, automatic process known as the "fight-or-flight" reaction, or the stress response. Minor hassles, serious challenges, painful memories, and even positive events can all cause your body to go into this stress response mode and prepare to either fight the threat or run from it. Once you recognize the sources of your stress, you will be able to anticipate and manage your stress more effectively.

Signs of Stress

Signs that you are under stress include:
  • Physical signs: headaches, muscle tension, grinding your teeth, sleep problems;
  • Behavioral signs: drinking too much, road rage, avoiding friends and family;
  • Emotional signs: being more irritable or depressed, mood swings, overreacting;
  • Problems with thinking: trouble concentrating, misinterpreting others, difficulty making decisions.

Effects of Long-term Stress

Some of the effects of long-term stress include:
  • Hypertension (high blood pressure)
  • Heart disease
  • Increased risk for infectious disease
  • Gastrointestinal disorders such as colitis and ulcers
  • Asthma
  • Depression and anxiety disorders
  • Poor performance on everyday tasks

How Can I Reduce Stress?

Strategies to reduce stress depend on whether you have control over the stressful situation. These strategies are described in detail on the following screens. Remember to seek support from friends and loved ones. They are some of your greatest allies against stress.

Stress-reduction Strategies If You Don't Have Control over the Stressful Situation

Emotion-Focused Activities
When faced with challenges outside your control try one of these strategies:
  • Listen to music, read a book, or watch a movie.
  • Get together with a friend.
  • Get some exercise.
  • Engage in spiritual activities.
  • Try yoga or meditation.
  • Take a nap or relaxing bath.
  • Write in a journal or diary.
  • Express your stress creatively (painting, playing a musical instrument).
  • Help others in need or volunteer.
  • Practice "anxiety surfing" (riding out anxieties until they naturally subside).
Mental Reframing
Everyone has a constant stream of thoughts running through their minds-this is called self-talk. Self-talk reflects your beliefs about the world and yourself. Negative self-talk can be a stressor. Mental reframing is a process that helps you interrupt negative self-talk and challenge it with more realistic, positive thoughts. There are five steps in mental reframing:
  1. Practice self-monitoring: monitor your thoughts to spot unhelpful or negative self-talk.
  2. Use the ABC model (A ctivating event + Beliefs = C onsequences): identify the situation (a ctivating event) and your b eliefs and thoughts about it. Recognize the c onsequence or affect of those thoughts on your feelings and behavior. Often your interpretation of a situation can lead to a stress reaction.
  3. Identify negative thought patterns: recognize when your beliefs about a situation are unhelpful or unrealistic. Common negative thought patterns include:
    1. All-or-Nothing Thinking: seeing things in black and white, "good" or "bad" terms when there is clearly a middle ground. An example is thinking that you are either perfect or a failure.
    2. Exaggeration: blowing the imagined consequences of an event out of proportion. You may see minor problems as "horrible" or "disastrous" but others may say you're making a mountain out of a molehill.
    3. Overgeneralization: taking an isolated experience and drawing general, often negative conclusions. For example, believing that if one person turns you down for a date that you will always be rejected or that you will never find the right person.
    4. Mind-Reading: assuming you know what another person is thinking. For example, thinking your friends don't want to hear about your deployment experiences, when they really just feel awkward asking about it.
  4. Challenge negative thoughts: look carefully for evidence that challenges negative or stressful self-talk. Ask yourself: Is my information reliable? Are my beliefs based on feelings rather than facts? Is there evidence for or against my stressful thoughts?
  5. Use positive self-talk: replace negative, stressful self-talk with positive, empowering self-talk.
Deep Breathing and Progressive Muscle Relaxation

When stressed, your breathing becomes fast and shallow and your muscles tense. You can interrupt this response and reduce your stress with deep breathing and muscle relaxation. To learn these techniques, try the Breathe2Relax mobile application or check out the Life Stress Workshop on afterdeployment.org.

Stress-reduction Strategies If You Do Have Control over the Stressful Situation

Nine-Step Problem-Solving Strategy
STEP 1: Recognize Signals of Stress. These signals include thoughts, feelings, behavior, and physical signs.
STEP 2: Define the Problem. Find the real problem and be specific about how it triggers your stress reaction.
STEP 3: Set a Goal. Set a specific goal for dealing with the stressor.
STEP 4: Brainstorm Possible Solutions. When you brainstorm, don't judge any of the solutions. Just let them flow and write them down. Be creative!
STEP 5: Evaluate Possible Solutions. Weigh the pros and cons of each possible solution you just brainstormed.
STEP 6: Choose the Best Possible Solutions Based on Pros and Cons. Give special consideration to the solution with the least cons.
STEP 7: Make a Plan and Try Your Solution. Create a step-by-step plan for achieving your solution. Set deadlines (time and date) for the completion of each step.
STEP 8: Assess the Solution. Ask yourself if you met your goal.
STEP 9: If the First Solution Didn't Work? Try another solution from your list.
