Families with Kids : Learn

Deployment Challenges for Parents

Service members with children face additional challenges when deploying. For single parents, or dual military families with both parents deployed, reliable childcare must be found. Some families relocate to be closer to additional childcare support, such as extended family, for the duration of the deployment. Even when children remain at home with the non-deployed parent, the effects of separation can be significant. The impacts of a deployment vary depending on the stage of the deployment cycle and the age of the child. By being aware of common reactions to deployments and learning ways to manage these reactions, every family member can help keep the family strong.

The Emotional Cycle of Deployment

The Emotional Cycle of Deployment has five stages. These stages span the entire deployment process, from initial notification to several months after returning home. Each deployment stage has unique emotional challenges.

The five stages of the Emotional Cycle of Deployment are:

  1. Pre-Deployment: From receiving deployment orders to the day of departure.
  2. Deployment: Typically the first month a service member is deployed.
  3. Maintenance: The majority of the deployment.
  4. Redeployment: The month before the deployed service member or parent is expected to return home.
  5. Post-deployment: Begins the moment the deployed service member is reunited with family and lasts for about two to five months of readjustment.

1. Pre-Deployment Stage: What to Expect

The pre-deployment stage often includes:

  • Negotiation: Children are often worried about their parent leaving and will try to talk them into staying home. They may negotiate by saying things like, "You don't really have to go, do you Mommy?" or, "Daddy, if I'm a really good girl will you stay home?"
  • Getting affairs in order: Many difficult preparations and decisions will need to be made before a deployment. These may include determining who will be responsible for tasks the deployed parent usually does, finding childcare, changing financial arrangements, etc.
  • Grief: It's common for families to feel intense sadness or loss when faced with life without a parent for a period of time.
  • Arguments: It's normal in stressful times for people to be more irritable and on edge.

Tips for the pre-deployment stage:

  • Explain why you are deploying. Make sure children understand that the upcoming deployment is part of your job. Kids need to be assured that their parent is not leaving because of anything they've done wrong. You can tell them you are going to help people who live far away. Remind your children that you love and appreciate them.
  • Family time. Don't ignore your children's needs while you're busy preparing for the deployment. Plan some fun family outings so everyone can spend quality time together before you leave.
  • Communicate expectations. Discuss how life will be different, but how you have faith in the children's ability to carry out responsibilities. Discussions like these will help kids realize they are an important part of the family, and they share a responsibility in helping the family survive.

2. Deployment Stage: What to Expect

Here are some common deployment stage experiences:

  • Mixed emotions: It's typical for all family members to deal with a roller coaster of emotions, including anger, sadness, fear, pride, excitement and anxiety.
  • Feeling numb: In the first month of the service member's absence, over-whelming emotions may lead to numb feelings in all family members.
  • Sadness: Family members may grieve the emotional and physical hole left in the household by the deployed service member.
  • Difficulty sleeping: Strong emotions and household changes make it difficult to relax enough to sleep.
  • Not feeling safe: When a parent departs, many children feel less safe and secure because of the change in family structure.

Tips for the deployment stage:

  • Spend extra time with your kids. The remaining parent/caregiver should spend extra time helping the kids during this transition. While it's important to be honest about the service member's deployment activities, it's okay to put a positive spin on the job to prevent the children from getting overly worried about their parent.
  • Pre-written, pre-stamped postcards. The deployed parent should take some pre-written, pre-stamped postcards while traveling. Receiving these will help your kids feel connected until more permanent communications are set up.
  • Continue family traditions and create new ones. Family routines and traditions help stabilize the family. So keep celebrating Friday pizza nights or Saturday outings even after a parent deploys. If you don't have any family traditions, now is a great time to start one! Encourage your children to talk about these kinds of events and activities with the deployed parent.

3. Maintenance Stage: What Families with Children Can Expect

Expect these common experiences in the maintenance stage:

  • New routines will be established: Remaining family members/caregivers will create new ways to run the household (housework, shopping, helping with homework, recreational activities) to adjust to the service member's absence and to feel "normal" again.
  • New sources of support are discovered: Family, friends, playgroups, community gatherings and religious/spiritual activities offer great connections and community for the family.
  • Less anxiety as the family gets used to the transition: As challenges arise, family members learn to cope with crises and make important decisions. The children will begin to feel safer and more secure.
  • Independence: While family members continue to miss the service member, they become independent and live happy, successful lives during the deployment.
  • Some problems might intensify: While most families adjust well, some worries, negative feelings, or difficult behaviors may continue. If these problems persist, struggling family members should seek help.

Tips for the maintenance stage:

  • Communicate with the deployed parent. As the family settles into their new routine, it is important that communication continues as much as possible with the deployed parent. Families should establish a communication schedule. A schedule makes it easier for the family to be available when the deployed parent calls and allows the family to anticipate and look forward to the calls.
  • Communicate on important days. Service members should make a special effort to contact the family on or around birthdays, anniversaries and other important dates. If this isn't possible, leaving a "back-up" card with a pre-written note inside will help everyone feel connected on special days.
  • Make plans for the family to grow together. Don't put life on hold until the deployed parent returns. Set specific goals for each of the children and yourself. Record and report the progress on each goal, so the deployed parent can be part of that progress by providing encouragement.
  • Help children understand deployment is temporary. You may not know exactly how long the deployment will last, but you can estimate when the deployed parent might return home. Once you know the return date, help your children create a "Countdown Calendar." The calendar should include events, such as holidays and family outings that help break the time a parent is deployed into shorter time periods.
  • Address children's questions and worries as truthfully as possible. Be open when talking with your children about the deployed parent and ask your children what prompted their questions or concerns. Reassure them about military training and protection, without making false promises about the safety or well-being of the deployed parent.
  • Keep a close relationship with the school and teachers. Schedule regular meetings with teachers, administrators and counselors. Let them know about the deployment so they understand the extra stress the child is facing. Educators can help you to recognize signs of stress in the children

4. Redeployment Stage: What to Expect

These are some common experiences in the re-deployment stage:

  • Anticipation of homecoming: Like the deployment stage, children may have mixed emotions during the homecoming period. This is common and normal.
  • Excitement: Excited children and spouses start making post-deployment plans ("When Mommy comes home we'll go to the carnival" or, "We'll watch lots of movies when Daddy gets back").
  • Nervousness: Some children worry about how change will affect their deployed parent. Children may ask questions like, "Will Mommy recognize me? Will I recognize her?" or, "Is Daddy going to be mad that we painted the house without him?"
  • Mixed emotions: It is normal for a child to confuse pride (about accomplishments while the parent was gone) and concern (that the parent may feel "left out" or not approve of changes made in their absence).

Tips for redeployment stage:

  • Keep routines. Keep your routine as normal as possible so children don't get anxious or feel ignored by preparations for the service member's return.
  • Include kids in the homecoming preparations. This will help them feel a part of the excitement of their parent's homecoming. Creating signs, baking a cake, making a card, and planning a party are great ways for kids to get involved.

5. Post-Deployment Stage: What to Expect

For many families, the idea of a reunion brings up feelings of happiness and excitement. For others, anticipating the reunion creates anxiety and uncertainty. If the family had problems before deployment, the homecoming may cause more stress, anxiety, and fear. Most families feel a mix of joy and anxiety about the homecoming.

Typically, families expect the homecoming to be a wonderful, problem-free time. Family members focus so much on surviving the deployment that they often forget to anticipate the challenges of homecoming. The reality of homecoming catches many families off-guard. Here is a typical cycle that families often experience following the reunion:

Honeymoon
  • Everything seems wonderful and there is a sense of euphoria and relief.
  • Focus is on family time - catching up and sharing experiences.
  • Conflicts and annoyances are avoided.
Adjustment
  • Pressures of daily life get stronger.
  • More conflict and irritation with each other.
  • Realization that changes need to be made.
  • Roles and expectations are changed and tested.
Integration
  • Adjustment in roles and expectations are made.
  • There is a decrease in conflict.
  • Disagreements are addressed rather than avoided.

Children react in unique ways to reuniting with a parent following a deployment. Some children react in ways that may upset the returning service member. Some children may not want to hug or kiss their returning parent. If the service member understands how children cope with homecoming, they'll avoid feeling hurt or upset by the child's reactions.

Tips for the post-deployment stage:
  • Recognize change. It's important for everyone to realize that things won't be exactly as they were before the deployment.
  • Reconnect. Attending family gatherings, enjoying events as a family, and spending "alone time" with your children will help everyone reconnect.
  • Clarify expectations. To avoid arguments and hurt feelings over changing roles and routines, talk openly about everyone's expectations.
  • Give it some time. Go slowly, be patient, and allow several months to reestablish close family connections. Returning parents should slowly rebuild trust, allowing relationships to heal naturally and without force.
  • Tell your kids how much you love them. Your kids need to hear you say how much you missed them and how happy you are to see them again.
  • Don't take it personally. Children often say and do things they don't really mean when they are upset or confused.
  • Listen sensitively to your children. Show them you are interested and ready to hear what they have to say.
  • Don't force your children to spend time with you. Let things happen naturally. Give your children time to readjust to you at their pace.
  • Spend time with each child individually. Make a date with each of them.
  • Limit your criticisms and judgments of your children.
  • Praise your children verbally and physically for big and small accomplishments. Tell them, "Nice job!" "Wow, I'm impressed," or "I am so proud of you." You can also give your child a hug, a high five, a pat on the back, or a special handshake. Children cannot be "spoiled" with praise.
  • Keep in mind that children mirror their parents. If you demonstrate strength and patience, it is likely that your child will be strong and patient, too.
  • Do not change the discipline your spouse used while you were away. Discipline methods should be changed only after you and your spouse have had a chance to discuss the issue privately.
  • Honor the relationship between your child and your spouse. Your children were your spouse's center of attention while you were away. Don't abruptly demote your children to the number two spot upon your return.
  • Learn about what's happened while you were away and what your child has accomplished. Together look at pictures, home movies, scrapbooks, art work, homework, awards, report cards, medals, trophies, and so on.
  • Bravery medals. Give your children "bravery medals" for being brave while you were away.
  • Show an interest in the everyday events of your children's lives. Find out what new interests your children developed while you were away. Don't tease your children about their interests, no matter how strange they may seem to you. Instead, get involved in their activities.
  • Remember - you are all adjusting!

New Parents

Service members who were away for the birth or first year of their baby's life will be coming home to a whole new family. There will be unique challenges in this situation that may include:

  • Feelings of jealousy and/or guilt. It's possible that you may feel jealous of the attention your partner gives your baby. Or you may feel guilty for being away during the pregnancy and the birth of your child. You need to accept two facts: (1) your baby's needs demand attention; and (2) your deployment was unavoidable. By moving past your negative feelings you can take an active role in caring for your baby and your partner.
  • Your partner may experience feelings of resentment toward you and even your baby for having to care for an infant alone. Express appreciation for everything done in your absence. Spend time together and talk about your feelings.
  • Your baby's needs come first, and those needs are expensive. Now that you're home, you won't get the special pay you may have received while deployed. Be prepared for a tighter budget.
  • Spend time with your other children. Older children may feel lost and angry with all of the changes. Spend quality time with them.

Single and Dual-Deployed Parents

Children of single parents and dual military households where both parents are deployed are often placed with trusted friends and family members for the duration of the deployment. If your children were placed with a caregiver outside the home, you'll face unique challenges. In particular, you may be anxious about the relationships that your children formed with their caregivers while you were away. You may also be worried about re-creating your own bond with your children. Here are some tips for a smoother transition with your kids:

  • Communicate openly and frequently with your children and their caregiver before and after you come home. Make sure the caregiver is actively involved in the transition. Separating from the caregiver may be difficult for your children. Keep your kids in touch with the caregiver through scheduled activities, especially in the first month after coming home.
  • Talk with the caregiver about your children's new routines and schedules. Learn the caregiver's rules and disciplining methods. Try to integrate this information into your own routines, schedules, and rules. This will help ease the transition for you and your children.

Soldiers Experiencing Combat Stress or PTSD

If you're having a tough time with combat stress reactions or you've been diagnosed with PTSD, you may be more worried and uncertain about spending time with your kids. You may worry about how you'll react to your children or how they'll react to you. You may also worry about "keeping it together" in front of your family. Your concerns are normal and they reflect your desire to be a good parent. Here are some additional ways to help the entire family when you come home:

  • Take care of yourself. Continue any treatments you've started or seek out appropriate treatment.
  • Teach your children about PTSD using age-appropriate language. Teach them what to expect. Assure them that you're working to overcome your problems. You can say things like this: "Sometimes when people have this, they want to be by themselves or they may be grouchy. I may be like that sometimes. I want you to know that even if I am, it's not your fault, and I love you very much." You may take your anger out on your children. Tell your children that your problems are not their fault. For example, you may yell at them because they made a mess or are making too much noise. If this happens, calm yourself down and apologize. Let your kids know your anger was not their fault. Tell them you're working hard and getting help to feel better.
  • Assure the children that their feelings are normal and valid. They may be angry, confused, sad, ashamed, or afraid. They may direct their negative emotions toward you. Patiently accept their reactions.
  • Seek support from friends, family, school, church, etc. You should not carry this burden yourself.
  • Keep enjoying fun activities. Stay involved in physical activities with your children, such as camping, playing ball, or hiking.

Soldiers with Physical Injuries

If you have sustained a brain injury or another physical injury while deployed, you may be worried about spending time with your children. If you need lots of medical attention, you may worry about how your children will react to the doctor's visits and the equipment. You may wonder how your kids will react to your injuries, especially if the injuries have changed the way you look or act. If you have an injury with no visible wounds, you may have difficulty communicating to your children why you can't do certain things even though you look okay. You may worry about how you'll relate to and play with your kids. These are normal concerns-they show you care about your relationship with your family.

If you did sustain an injury, here are some ways you can help both yourself and your family after returning home:

  • Take care of yourself, physically and mentally. It's important to continue your medical care, or seek behavioral health support if you suffer from combat stress, depression, or anxiety.
  • Before you return home, discuss the injuries with your kids over the phone. Send them a picture of you or talk over a webcam so they know what you look like with your injury. Involve their caregiver in preparing your children for your return home.
  • After returning, teach your children about your injuries. Use age-appropriate language. If appropriate, show your children your injury. Let them know what to expect as you heal. Involve your spouse in these conversations, too.
  • Tell your children that their feelings are normal and valid. They may be angry, confused, sad, ashamed, or afraid. They also may be proud of your injuries. They may direct their negative emotions toward you. Or they may try to shield you from everything negative. Whatever their reactions, be patient and accept their emotions.

Responses to Separation at Different Ages

There are some common reactions to separation depending on a child's age. Each child will have their own unique reaction, but learning guidelines can help you understand your child's reaction.

Infants (ages 0-1):
  • Infants must be held and actively nurtured. If the caregiver gets depressed during the service member's deployment, the infant may fuss a lot or have less energy and interest in what's going on around them. They may also lose weight by refusing to eat. In these cases, a pediatrician should evaluate the baby right away. The caregiver should also monitor their emotional and physical health and may need to seek professional care if they have difficulty adjusting or are feeling depressed.
  • What can the deployed parent do? By supporting the caregiver, the deployed service member can help care for an infant negatively affected by the separation. Letters, e-mails, postcards and photos are powerful ways to communicate love, affection, and support to the caregiver who may be lonely and depressed.
Toddlers (ages 1-3):
  • Toddlers tend to copy the attitudes and behaviors of their caregivers. If the caregiver is coping well, the child will tend to do well. If the caregiver has emotional difficulties, a toddler may become gloomy, tearful, throw tantrums, or develop sleep problems. Children usually respond positively to more attention, hugs and holding hands. The caregiver might benefit from sharing their experiences with others facing similar challenges.
  • What can the deployed parent do? Toddlers can identify people in photos and understand that a person still exists even when they are not present. The deployed parent should send updated photos and communicate directly with the child through letters, e-mails and postcards. Some of the deployed parent's letters can be read by the caregiver much like a bedtime story.
Preschoolers (ages 3-6):
  • Preschoolers with negative reactions to a parent's deployment may act younger than their age. They may have difficulty with potty training, begin bed-wetting, revert to baby talk and thumb-sucking, want to sleep in the parents' bed, and so forth. They may also seem irritable, depressed, aggressive, clingy, and frightened that the caregiver will leave them. The caregiver should reassure children with extra attention and by maintaining family routines. Answers to their questions about deployment should be brief and matter-of-fact. This will help to reduce the anxiety caused by a child's overactive imagination.
  • What can the deployed parent do? Younger preschoolers respond well to visual stimulation. Sending cards, photos, funny images, and colorful pictures may really cheer them up. If you have an older preschooler learning to read, you can write short, simple notes to help them practice reading. These notes will help them feel connected and make them proud of their abilities as well.
School-age children (ages 6-12):
  • School-age children may whine, complain, become aggressive, or act out their feelings. They may focus on the deployed parent missing a key event, such as "If Daddy really loved me, he'd be here for my birthday." Some signs of difficulty adjusting may include: sleep problems, loss of interest in school, not eating or eating too much, and not playing with friends. They should be encouraged to talk about their feelings and will need more attention (hugs, joint activities, help with homework) than usual. Stick to family routines as much as possible to help them feel more safe and secure. Keeping school-age children involved in extra-curricular activities is also a good idea.
  • What can the deployed parent do? Celebrate birthdays and other important events, like good report cards or sports achievements. Buy birthday presents, cards and other gifts before deploying. This way, your child will feel special, opening something you picked out. Writing detailed letters directly to the older school-aged child will also help them feel important and connected to you.
Teenagers (ages 13-18):
  • Teenagers may rebel, fight, and seek attention in unhealthy ways. They may lose interest in school, peers, and activities. They also run an increased risk of experimenting with unsafe sex, alcohol, and drugs. Since teens often deny their problems and worries, the caregiver must stay actively involved and talk about life issues with teens regularly. If a teenager's grades suffer, be patient and look for ways to help improve things. Encourage teens to get involved in sports or other social activities that can provide some structure to their life. Find ways to give them more family responsibilities so they feel important and needed.
  • What can the deployed parent do? E-mails and letters show that deployed parents understand a teenager's frustration. Through e-mails and letters, you can also explain your expectations of your teen as a young adult. Teenagers need to understand you expect them to act maturely, help around the house, and support the rest of the family. You should also encourage your teen to express their feelings in letters and e-mails to you. Teens generally respond well to a balance of love and boundaries.

The Adjustment of the Remaining Parent or Caregiver

Deployment often creates stressful circumstances for the remaining parent/caregiver. They may have to take over new roles and responsibilities. For example, they may have to adjust to a reduced income or separation from close friends and a supportive community if they relocated. Deployment may even create or increase tension in the relationship with the deployed parent.

Children look to their caregivers as examples. If caregivers struggle to cope with these new demands, the children will likely struggle as well. Children adjust better when they see their caregivers cope with the separation, manage the household, and stay involved socially.

Helping Caregivers Cope during Stressful Times

If you've flown on an airplane, you've heard the flight attendant tell parents, in the event of an emergency, to secure their own oxygen mask before securing a child's oxygen mask. In the same way, caregivers should first manage their own care and support before addressing the needs of the children.

Here are some self-care tips for the caregiver.
  • Schedule time for yourself. Create a schedule with special time for you. You'll have more energy and be in better spirits if you take time to exercise, nap, spend time alone, or take time with a good supportive friend. These activities can be brief, but planned. Thank the kids for giving you time to yourself. Let them know how much better you feel.
  • Communicate with other families. Join a support group of parents with deployed spouses. Talking with people who are facing similar experiences helps!
  • Tell the children why you are upset. Children tend to focus more of their concern on their caregiver. If children know why their at-home parent is emotional, they can accept the situation much more easily. *NOTE: Don't share all the details of your concerns or problems with your children. Use good judgment and share just the things that will ease your child's worries.

When to Seek Help for a Child

Sometimes your best efforts are not enough to prevent a child from having problems. The decision to seek professional help for a child can be difficult and painful. Talking openly and honestly with the child about the decision will help. It also helps to talk with the child's doctor, teachers, clergy members, or other adults who know your child well. These symptoms may mean your child needs professional help:

Preschoolers:

  • Refusing to go to daycare, sleep, or take part in normal activities.
  • Fidgeting or constant movement beyond regular playing.
  • Frequent nightmares.
  • Recurrent, unexplainable temper tantrums.

Elementary Age Children:

  • Lower grades.
  • Increased difficulties with peers at school.
  • Refusing to go to school, sleep, or take part in normal activities.
  • Fidgeting or constant movement beyond regular playing.
  • Frequent disobedience or aggression (for longer than 6 months), and resistance to authority.

Pre-Adolescents and Adolescents:

  • Lower grades.
  • Inability to cope with problems and daily activities.
  • Marked changes in sleeping or eating habits.
  • Frequent physical complaints.
  • Acting out sexually.
  • Depression or persistent negative moods and attitudes or suicidal thoughts.
  • Abuse of alcohol or drugs.
  • Intense, irrational fear of becoming overweight.
  • Frequent nightmares.
  • Threats of self-harm or harm to others.
  • Self-injury or self-destructive behavior, like cutting on arms or legs.
  • Frequent outbursts of anger and aggression.
  • Threats to run away.
  • Anti-social or criminal behavior such as resisting authority, truancy, stealing, or vandalism.

Where to Find Help for Your Child

If you're worried about your child's emotions or behavior, talk to:

  • Friends.
  • Family members.
  • A spiritual or religious counselor.
  • School counselor.
  • Pediatrician or family doctor.

If your child needs professional help, here are some options to find a qualified therapist or counselor for your child:

  • Military hospital or medical center.
  • MilitaryOneSource.com.
  • TRICARE.
  • Employee Assistance Program through an employer.
  • Local hospitals or medical centers with behavioral health services.
  • National Advocacy Organizations (National Alliance for the Mentally Ill; Federation of Families for Children's Mental Health; National Mental Health Association, Anxiety Disorders Association of America).
  • National professional organizations (American Psychological Association; American Academy of Pediatrics; American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry; American Psychiatric Association).
