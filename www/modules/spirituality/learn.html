Spirituality : Learn

What Is Spirituality?

Spirituality has been defined in numerous ways. These can include a belief in a higher power operating the universe that is greater than onself, a sense of interconnectedness with all living creatures, or an awarenss of the purpose and meaning of life and the development of personal values. Spirituality is also about a connection with a divine presence, within or outside of any religious affiliation. Some of the ways people describe spirituality include:

  • Life's meaning and importance.
  • Values, beliefs, and ethics.
  • Awareness of life beyond yourself.
  • Connection between you, others, and God;
  • Reflecting on life, and a sense of "deep self" or soul.

What Is Spiritual Fitness?

Spiritual fitness means having a set of beliefs and values that guide and give life meaning. Spiritual practices tend to improve coping skills and social support, foster feelings of optimism and hope, promote healthy behavior, reduce feelings of depression and anxiety, and encourage a sense of relaxation. By alleviating stressful feelings and promoting healing ones, spirituality can positively influence immune, cardiovascular, hormonal and nervous systems. Studies have also found that veterans who have an active spiritual practice report fewer and less severe PTSD symptoms. Spiritual fitness can also bond people through love, forgiveness, and compassion.

Like physical fitness, spiritual fitness must be tended to and practiced. Some people stay spiritually fit by participating in organized religion. Others stay spiritually fit by spending time in nature, volunteering, creating something useful, or playing with a pet.

People who struggle in their spirituality or relationship with God may struggle in other areas of their life as well.

War and Spirituality

Many service members think about spiritual issues during and after deployment. It's normal to look at the world differently after facing danger and death. Some gain a stronger relationship with God. Others may question their beliefs or feel spiritually empty. You may wonder:

  • "Was that death God's will?"
  • "Is there any meaning or purpose to the awful things I saw or experienced?"
  • "Am I condemned, or a bad person, because of my actions?"
  • "Can I be forgiven?"
  • How can I trust anyone after what I know people are capable of?"
  • "Why did this happen to me? What did I do to deserve this?"
  • "Why did my buddy die and not me? It should've been me."

Spiritual Alienation and Isolation

Spiritual alienation means moving away from what is spiritually meaningful, sacred, or divine. Reactions to trauma, such as questioning the meaning of events, doubting your beliefs, and feeling betrayed by God or life itself, can lead to feeling spiritually alone and alienated. Feeling disconnected or isolated from others often goes hand in hand with spiritual alienation.

Feeling alone and disconnected is common after a traumatic event. You may feel like no one understands what you've been through. After being injured or seeing others hurt or killed, you may feel angry or vulnerable and have problems trusting people. You may want to withdraw or isolate yourself, but without at least a little trust in something or someone, life can get lonely and bitter.

You may feel even more disconnected and alone if you think "all-or-nothing" thoughts that put things in black and white terms. Remember that there is usually some gray area. Instead of thinking "The world is never safe" or "I can't trust anyone," try thinking "Something bad happened, but that doesn't mean the whole world is unsafe" or "I can't trust everyone, but I can trust some people with some things." Remind yourself that there are people you can trust and connect with them. Connecting with others is part of what nourishes a person's spiritual life. Connecting gives us a sense of meaning and purpose.

Tips for Beating Isolation

Being around others is important for spiritual and personal well-being. Withdrawing from people affects spiritual fitness because without connection, life can feel meaningless. You can beat isolation by:

  • Making plans ahead of time to socialize with others. Stick to the plan even if your impulse is to be alone.
  • Committing to leaving the house for at least thirty minutes a day and go to a location where you will find other people (grocery store, post office, bank, etc.).
  • Spending time with people you trust in places you feel comfortable.
  • Being in places where you're around people but don't have to interact much, like a church service or concert.
  • Going on short outings. Gradually increase how long you stay out with each outing.
  • Doing things with people where your attention is on the activity instead of the person, like volunteering, playing a game or sport, working on a project, or going to a movie.
  • Increasing your comfort zone and taking small risks with someone you trust.

Reconnecting with Your Spirituality

Learning to make sense of what happened can rebuild your sense of purpose and reconnect you with your spirituality. Connecting with your spirituality improves your quality of life and helps you recover and rebuild healthier life conditions. Things you can do to restore some balance and help you to find meaning in traumatic events include:

  • Share your thoughts, feelings and questions with counselors, chaplains, or someone you trust. They can help you rebuild your beliefs and find meaning. Talk about what led you to stop trusting in the first place. For many people, the anger and fear begin to dissolve once they talk and feel heard.
  • Become more aware of what you say to yourself. Weed out the unrealistic thoughts and beliefs and plant thoughts that are more accurate.
  • Spend time thinking, expressing, and making sense of the experience through talking, writing, art, music, or other means.
  • Practice your spirituality to feel more connected and focused on what is important to you.
  • Spend time in nature or with animals.
