Mild Traumatic Brain Injury : Learn

Sleep Basics

An injury to the head is called a traumatic brain injury or "TBI." But more than just a hit to the head, a TBI has the potentional to affect brain functioning. In other words, the brain temporarily stops working in its usual way. A TBI might cause an individual to become dazed, or experience a period of unconsciousness. There may be difficulty remembering what happened right before or after the injury.

The severity of a TBI can range from mild (feeling "dazed" or being unconscious for a short period of time) to severe ( a longer period of unconsciousness or inability to remember the injury). Individuals who experience head injuries have described the experience as "seeing stars," or "getting my bell rung," or "getting knocked out."

Are Concussions The Same As TBI

While the terms "concussion" and "mild traumatic brain injury," (mTBI) mean the same thing, some brain injuries can be more severe than a concussion (therefore more severe than a mild TBI). An individual who has been concussed may try to walk off the effects of the concussion and continue with activities. Walking off the effects of a concussion has the potential to increase the risk of further injury.

If you've been diagnosed with a TBI, your provider has probably discussed the level of severity of your injury. The Head Injury Severity table below outlines the differences between mild, moderate, and severe. Note that these severity factors are measured at the time of injury, not minutes, days, or months after the fact.

Head Injury Severity

  • Mild/Concussion
    • Responsiveness at Time of Injury (able to open eyes, speak move, etc.)
      • Fully awake or only small difficulties
    • Alteration in Consciousness (length of time being dazed, confused, or not thinking clearly)
      • 24 hours or less
    • Loss of Consciousness (length of time spent unconscious)
      • Less than 30 minutes
    • Post-traumatic Amnesia (how long after the injury until having the first memory)
      • Less than 24 hours
  • Moderate
    • Responsiveness at Time of Injury (able to open eyes, speak move, etc.)
      • Some functions working and some not working
    • Alteration in Consciousness (length of time being dazed, confused, or not thinking clearly)
      • More than 24 hours
    • Loss of Consciousness (length of time spent unconscious)
      • 30 minutes to 24 hours
    • Post-traumatic Amnesia (how long after the injury until having the first memory)
      • 1-7 days
  • Severe
    • Responsiveness at Time of Injury (able to open eyes, speak move, etc.)
      • Coma/unresponsive
    • Alteration in Consciousness (length of time being dazed, confused, or not thinking clearly)
      • More than 24 hours
    • Loss of Consciousness (length of time spent unconscious)
      • More than 24 hours
    • Post-traumatic Amnesia (how long after the injury until having the first memory)
      • More than 7 days

How is TBI Diagnosed?

First, only medically trained specialists can determine if someone has a TBI. In fact, a concussion or TBI can be difficult to diagnose, especially if there are no visible or external signs of the injury. Concussions are most commonly diagnosed via an interview by a medically trained specialist. Doctors may also use CT scans, x-rays of the skull, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and single photon emission computed tomography (SPECT). However, 85% of concussions are not detected using these tests. Many clinicians rely on interview data, meeting with the patient and his family and friends, along with monitoring symptoms with questionaires and tests.

So How Long Does It Take To Heal?

The effects of a concussion are usually temporary and disappear after a few weeks. When symptoms do not go away naturally, there may be complicating factors, including other physical injuries and even mood and anxiety problems such as depression and post-traumatic stress. Generally, the longer the period of unconsciousness at the time of the injury, the longer the period of recovery.

TBI symptoms that are moderate in severity may linger for months. In military settings, many service members with a moderate TBI can return to active duty, while others will need to retire from the military. For the best outcome, moderate TBI requires treatment by a team of medical providers. Symptoms can generally be managed with a regimen of medication, counseling, and learning strategies.

Severe TBI often results in long-term nursing care and difficulty with independent fuctioning.

What Happens After A Concussion

The difficulties associated with a concussion can be grouped into three categories:

  1. Problems with thinking ability - memory, concentration, focusing.
  2. Problems with moods and behaviors - depression, anxiety, irritability, sleep issues.
  3. Problems with body and senses - headache, fatigue, vision problems, dizziness

Because the signs and symptoms of a concussion can be subtle, they can be overlooked by patients, family members, and health care professionals. Concussions can be different from one person to the next, in this respect, some individuals experience many symptoms while others experience fewer. Having multiple symptoms doesn't necessarily mean having a more difficult recovery - many factors influence recovery time from a concussion, including flexibility, goal-setting, and social support.

Will a TBI Affect My Day-To-Day Life?

Your day-to-day life may be affected very little by the concussion. But understanding the many symptoms that occur following a concussion is crucial to being able to take control of your recovery. "Symptoms" are just labels for how the mind and body are working less than optimally, the important determination is identifying how the symptoms affect day-to-day activities (secondary problems)

What Can I Do To Thrive After My Concussion?

Following a concussion, it's best to be realistic about how quickly you'll get better and what must be done to support your recovery. Some hear the term "brain injury" and think "brain damage." Framing the problem this way can lead to feeling hopeless. In reality, brain injuries are like many other injuries. For example, to treat a broken foot, a physician will recommend crutches and instruct that weight-bearing should be avoided until the foot is healed. The same idea applies with concussion - the brain needs time to rest and heal.
