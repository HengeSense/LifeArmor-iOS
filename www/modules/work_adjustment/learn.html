Challenges in Returning to Work after Deployment

Deployments interrupt your normal work life. Some deployments involve intense mission focus and the threat of combat. Other deployments can feel disorienting with too much time after duty and none of the usual after work responsibilities of home. Some deployments cycle from boredom to combat to boredom and force you to drastically adjust your mind-set without warning. When you return from deployment, you may need to change your expectations and behavior to adjust back to "normal" work. Some common difficulties in transitioning back into the workplace include:

  • Feeling the job is pointless or boring. Returning service members sometimes report that work seems pointless or dull compared to their deployment missions. Service members who endured difficult deployments may feel anxious, irritable, or on edge. Co-workers or supervisors may not share the same sense of urgency about daily tasks. These feelings can lead to a negative outlook toward the workplace and co-workers.
  • Feeling on edge in crowded, busy work environments. During deployments, survival sometimes depends on thinking, feeling, and acting automatically. However, these same automatic responses, which may have saved your life in the war-zone, can be counterproductive back in the workplace. Many worksites are busy places with many people coming, going, and moving around. If you are uneasy in crowds, you may find being at work exhausting and stressful. Constantly feeling on high alert may also result in being easily startled.
  • Low self-esteem at work. Some service members return home feeling depressed or guilty about things that happened during deployment. People who judge themselves negatively often suffer from low self-esteem. This can cause trouble concentrating and completing tasks. Low self-esteem can also make you feel that you're not worth your boss or coworkers investing time or energy in and may make you want to quit before trying to find solutions.
  • Trusting your coworkers and supervisor. You may find that you don't trust people who have no deployment or military experience. Some service members admit they don't really trust anyone. At work, trust problems make accomplishing tasks through teamwork more difficult.

The Importance of Returning to Work

Work can greatly impact the kind of success a returning service member experiences. Successful workplace reintegration can result in:

  • Greater self-esteem and sense of well-being.
  • A sense of purpose and contribution.
  • Improved mood through work achievements.
  • Stability and routine that lets you feel safe to plan for the future.

Difficult workplace reintegration can result in:

  • Feelings of isolation and failure.
  • Increased stress, frustration, and worry.
  • Difficulties with memory and focus.
  • Problems in relationships with job peers or supervisors.
  • Difficulty sleeping or waking.
  • Anger or irritability.
  • Low self-esteem.
  • Problems with drugs or alcohol.

Strategies to Successfully Reintegrate to Work

There are things you can do to improve your return to the workplace. Many of these strategies can also help your post-deployment reintegration with your family and friends. These strategies include:

  • Maintaining your physical health.
  • Talking about your deployment and its impact on you.
  • Asking for support.

Maintaining Your Physical Health

Like a car, your body is vulnerable to problems when not maintained properly. Physical health problems can affect your moods, concentration, sleep, relationships, and job performance. If you're having trouble staying physically healthy:

  • Stick to a sleep routine.
  • Exercise daily.
  • Take timeout to relax or do enjoyable activities.
  • Take medications as prescribed.
  • See a doctor for illnesses or injuries when necessary.
  • Eat healthy foods regularly throughout the day.
  • If you drink, do so in moderation.
  • Try to quit smoking or using other tobacco products.

Talking About Your Deployment

Discussing deployment experiences is your decision. You don't have to reveal anything you don't want to. Know that people will be curious about your experiences and may ask you about them. Coworkers may ask difficult questions about your deployment. Identify the things that will upset you, and be ready to cope with them. Remember that strong anger or intimidation by either person in the conversation is not appropriate at work. Even if you're provoked, you don't have to behave in a way that gets you reprimanded or dismissed.

If you don't want to talk about your deployment or get asked questions that provoke or upset you, it can be useful to have a few prepared responses, such as:

  • "No offense, but I don't like to talk about my war experiences."
  • "I'm not comfortable answering those questions right now."
  • "Let's talk about something else."

Asking for Support

Your ability to ease into the workplace rests in part on your willingness to communicate your struggles. If you are having difficulty readjusting to work, it may be helpful to discuss this with your coworkers or supervisor. Stress not only impacts your work performance, but may be affecting your coworkers too. If you work in the civilian sector, your coworkers and supervisor may not know how to respond to deployment-related issues and they may appear uncomfortable. Most of the time, it's simply a lack of understanding of the military, war, and combat-related stress.

Tips for asking for support:

  • Talk with a trusted counselor, family member, or friend FIRST. The two of you can weigh the pros and cons of sharing concerns with your supervisor or coworkers.
  • Focus. What needs to be said? Focus on problems that affect the workplace, and the steps you will take to manage those problems.
  • Consider possible reactions. Will they understand? Will they listen? If you think they won't support you, then be prepared for that, too.
  • Carefully choose who to talk to. Maybe just your direct supervisor needs to know. No matter who it is, make sure you have good reasons to share with them.
  • Focus On Work Success. Emphasize the importance of succeeding at work, and doing the things that will support work performance. Tell them the conversation will explain the workplace problems you're having and how they can support you.
  • Remember that people want to know about your deployment experiences. You may get some uncomfortable questions, but its best to assume that people mean well with their questions.
  • Don't assume confidentiality. Consider the potential risks if something you discuss privately is revealed to unintended parties.
  • Keep in mind that a lot of people outside the military don't know much about war, trauma, depression, deployment, or the military. Be patient when discussing your personal experiences.
  • Try a brief conversation and check the reaction. You don't have to share everything at once.
  • Stay Positive. Explain things in a positive way. This puts everyone at ease and reassures them
