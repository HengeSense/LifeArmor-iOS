Anger : Learn

What Is Anger?

Anger is an emotion characterized by antagonism toward someone or something you feel has deliberately done you wrong. Anger can be a good thing. It can give you a way to express negative feelings, for example, or motivate you to find solutions to problems. Alternatively, excessive anger can cause problems. Increased blood pressure and other physical changes associated with anger make it difficult to think straight and can harm your physical and mental health.

When Does Anger Become A Problem?

Anger is a completely normal, usually healthy, human emotion. When your anger gets out of control and turns destructive, it can lead to problems, problems at work, in your personal relationships, and in the overall quality of your life. Anger can make you feel as though you're at the mercy of an unpredictable and powerful emotion. When your anger becomes too intense or leads to violent behavior your heart rate and blood pressure go up, as do the levels of your energy hormones, adrenaline and noradrenalin. Additional health concerns can include: high cholesterol, muscle and joint pain, heart disease, chronic pain and even early death.

What Is The Difference Between Being Aggressive and Being Assertive?

The instinctive, natural way to express anger is to respond aggressively. Aggression is a behavior and can mean acting in a forceful, dominating way and can include physical violence or verbal threats. Being aggressive can have temporary benefits to include: quickly met demands from people who fear your physical and verbal threats, the release of built up physical emotion and the feeling of respect from others when they give in to you aggressive demands.

Assertiveness is also a type of behavior but is a mode of communication that affirms a person's point of view without threatening the rights of another. Expressing your angry feelings in an assertive, not aggressive, manner is the healthiest way to express anger. Being assertive doesn't mean being pushy or demanding; it means being respectful of yourself and others.

How Do I Prevent My Anger From Becoming Aggressive Or Violent?

You can manage your anger by familiarizing yourself with the three phases of the "Aggression Cycle" in addition to using a 0-10 "Anger Meter" scale. Using this anger meter scale will offer you a way to rate the intensity of your anger. The three phases of the Aggression Cycle are as follows:

  • The Escalation Phase: During the escalation phase, anger is building. Any anger meter scores rated below a "10" means you are currently in this phase.
  • The Explosion Phase: During the explosion phase you have reached a "10" on the anger meter and may express your emotions with verbal or physical aggression. This may include cursing, breaking or hitting things and fighting.
  • The Post-Explosion Phase: The post-explosion phase is the result of the explosion phase and you may experience feelings of guilt, shame, and regret after hurting friends, coworkers or significant others and you now be faced with further struggles at work or legal problems.

How Can I Control My Anger?

Anger's physical cues can range from an increase in heart rate, feeling hot or flushed to muscle tension. It's physically impossible to be both angry and relaxed at the same time. When you recognize these anger cues in yourself try practicing a deep breathing exercise like Breathe2Relax which is a mobile application that can be downloaded to your smartphone or slowly repeat a calm word or phrase such as "take it easy". Practice these techniques daily and learn to use them automatically when you're in a tense situation.

What Can I Do Before "It's Too Late"?

When you get angry, challenge any negative thoughts contributing toward that anger or try saying the following phrases to yourself:

  • "I'm going to stop thinking these thoughts because they make me feel worse."
  • "I'm just going to take it easy."
  • "I'm going to take a deep breath and count to 10."
  • "I know that getting angry won't solve this problem."
  • "I'm going to let it go."
