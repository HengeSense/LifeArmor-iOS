Anxiety and Worry: The Basics

Anxiety is a mix of thoughts, emotions, and physical reactions. People describe experiencing "butterflies" or "knots" in their bellies, or a pounding heart, or chest tightness. Anxiety can be useful, signaling the body to go "on alert" and prepare for action. Worry is the thinking part of anxiety in which the worrier regularly predicts the occurrence of negative or catastrophic events in his life or in the life of loved ones. Worried thinking often leads to anxiety's physical symptoms (rapid heartbeat, restlessness, disrupted sleep, etc.)

When Does Anxiety Become A Problem?

Anxious feelings may be so uncomfortable that an individual will do anything to avoid the feelings, including stopping or changing every day activities. Anxiety becomes an "anxiety disorder" when it regularly interferes with common daily activities. For example, severe anxiety can prevent a person from going to work or from engaging in meaningful events with friends and family.

The body will also react to anxiety that lacks an actual "cause." For example, during a panic attack, there may be physical symptoms such as dizziness, blurred vision, numbness, tingling, stiff muscles, and breathlessness, possibly all in response to an imagined threat. Even though there is no actual threat, the body's reactions are interpreted as life-threatening even though they are not dangerous. This is called catastrophic thinking. An individual with catastrophic thinking can get caught up in a vicious cycle: physical symptoms lead to anxiety, and anxiety 'feeds' back into the physical symptoms, worsening the intensity of those symptoms. This has the net effect of increasing the tendency to make catastrophic predictions.

What Are Common Types of Anxiety Disorders?

Here are some types of anxiety disorders:
  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) is defined as worrying constantly about small or large concerns. GAD is usually accompanied by physical symptoms such as restlessness, fatigue, irritabilty, and trouble sleeping.
  • Panic Disorder is characterized by recurrent panic attacks which involve sudden feelings of fear and terror for no obvious reason. Panic attacks can happen without warning and are usually accompanied by physical symptoms such as difficulty breathing, rapid heartbeat, dizziness, and chest pain.
  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) develops following the experience of a trauma in which severe physical harm occurred or was threatened. Individuals may have persistent frightening thoughts of the trauma, feel emotionally dampened or numb, or easily startled.
  • Social Anxiety Disorder is characterized by marked anxiety in social situations due to excessive fear of being criticized or scrutinized by others.
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) is characterized by recurrent, unwanted, and intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and or repetitive behaviors (compulsions). Intrusive thoughts may be of a specific danger or an unrealistic fear of harming oneself or others.

What Causes Anxiety Problems?

Anxiety is associated with a family history of the disorder, brain chemistry, and the occurrence of traumatic events.
  • Family History Genetics. Often, anxiety disorders are hereditary. If parents or other close relatives have had problems with anxiety, children are more likely to develop an anxiety disorder.
  • Learning. Children learn how to behave by observing others. Parents who are worriers and who regularly predict negative outcomes increase the probability that their children will learn this style of thinking and develop anxious behaviors.
  • Brain Chemistry. Thoughts and feelings are regulated by chemical messengers in the brain, called neurotransmitters. Neurotransmitters that are out of balance can increase anxiety symptoms.
  • Traumatic Events. A traumatic event, such as witnessing a death, or being in combat, can lead to anxiety.

What Makes Anxiety Worse?

Recognizing that life circumstances can worsen anxiety is an important first step toward reducing anxiety. Here are some situations that can worsen anxiety.
  • Stress. Stress affects everyone, but overwhelming stress can result in anxiety, especially for people who are vulnerable or actively diagnosed with an anxiety disorder.
  • Caffeine. Stimulants, such as caffeine found in coffee, tea, soda, chocolate, and some over-the-counter medications, can aggravate anxiety.
  • Unrealistic Thinking. Anxiety often affects an individual's ability to think clearly and discriminate between rational and irrational thoughts.
  • Poor Coping Skills. Anxiety is unlikely to resolve or improve when effective coping skills are absent.

Do Individuals With Anxiety Experience Other Problems?

Yes, in fact, anxiety is often accompanied by other conditions, such as:
  • Alcohol Abuse. Many people who feel anxious use alcohol to calm themselves, especially in social situations. However, alcohol can worsen anxiety and panic.
  • Depression. Approximately half of individuals diagnosed with depression also present with anxiety.
  • Eating Disorders. Some people who experience anxiety eat, and potentially overeat, to calm themselves.
  • Medical Conditions. Anxiety symptoms such as racing thoughts or a rapid heartbeat can interfere with sleep.

How Can I Manage My Anxiety?

There are many strategies that can help with worry and anxiety. Muscle relaxation, imagery, and breathing exercises calm the mind and reduce the physical symptoms of anxiety. Other strategies are designed to teach worriers to change catastrophic thinking oso that imagining "worse case" scenarios and predictions of disaster are reduced. Here are some helpful treatments:
  • Psychotherapy
  • Medication
  • Progressive Muscle Relaxation
  • Guided Imagery Visualization
  • Diaphragmatic Breathing
  • Distraction
