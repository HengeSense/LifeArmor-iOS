Tobacco Use in the Military

In the 1980's, more than half of service members smoked (51%). While that number declined by 2005 (32%), the overall percentage was still above the national rate of smoking among adults. And nearly 30% of Veteran Affairs (VA) patients smoke. Service members also use other tobacco products, including chewing tobacco and snuff. These smokeless tobacco products are just as addictive as cigarettes.

Why Should I Quit Smoking?

People start smoking, and continue to smoke, and even quit smoking, for many different reasons. Whatever led to the decision to quit, quitting has major benefits.
  • Health problems: smoking-related illnesses such as heart and lung disease and cancer take a toll on physical readiness and quality of life.
  • Work-related problems: smokers are more likely to miss work, have poorer motor and perceptual skills, and less endurance.
  • Financial problems: the Department of Defense spends an estimated $875 million annually on smoking-related health care and financial losses due to work absences and decreased productivity. Veterans Affairs (VA) estimates that health care costs for smoking-related problems will be as much as $2 billion per year within the next decade.

What Is The Best Way To Quit?

While nicotine addiction can be hard to break, success is improved with preparation.
  • Get yourself 'psyched up'
  • Set a date to quit
  • Talk to your doctor
  • Tell family, friends, coworkers

How Are Tobacco Use and Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) Related?

The rate of smoking among service members with PTSD doubles that of civilians. Service members and veterans with PTSD also smoke more heavily than service members and veterans who do not have a PTSD diagnosis. Many service members with PTSD claim that smoking reduces their stress. But, while smoking may cause a short-term decrease in anxiety, research shows that nicotine can make PTSD symptoms worse. In fact, nicotine stresses the nervous system just like a life threatening situation: there is an increase in heart rate, blood pressure, and adrenaline, and parts of the body no longer get adequate blood supply.

Will I Go Through Withdrawal When I Quit?

Nicotine is the addictive chemical in cigarettes and other tobacco products. "Withdrawal" can occur when a substance that the body has become dependent on, such as nicotine, is discontinued. Almost all people who quit nicotine will experience some kind of withdrawal symptoms. The longer a person has smoked or has used smokeless tobacco, the more severe the withdrawal symptoms may be. Withdrawal symptoms tend to be strongest the first week after quitting; as a result, this is the period when many people start using tobacco again. Common withdrawal symptoms including feeling hungry, having difficulty sleeping, and feeling frustrated or mad. Most phsyical symptoms (hunger, sleep difficulty) disappear after a couple of weeks. Emotional dependence can last longer.

How Do I Handle Cravings?

Almost everyone has cravings soon after they have quit using tobacco. Because individuals learn to associate life experiences with tobacco use, cravings come to be triggered by people, places, objects, situations, times of day, and feelings. Breaking the "vicious cycle" between triggers and the act of smoking involves stopping the craving dead in its tracks. Do the following:

  • Identify triggers by paying attention to different experiences that trigger the urge to use.
  • Avoid these triggers. For example, avoid places such as bars where other people are using.
  • Deal with triggers without smoking or using tobacco. Keep your mouth and hands busy by chewing gum or hard candy, or by calling a friend.

What If I Smoke Or Use Tobacco After I Quit?

Setbacks usually occur within the first three months of quitting. If you have a setback, accept that you've gotten off track, avoid being too hard on yourself, and get back on track! And while it's common to have a few setbacks, it's best to not use 'every now and then,' no matter how long it's been since you quit. Even though occasional use may seem harmless, occasional use can lead to more frequent use.

Where Can I Find Support To Help Me Quit?

People who have social support who are engaged in accessing resources are 50% more likely to quit than those who lack support. To help you quit, try the following resources:

  • The workplace military installations and medical facilites have smoking cessation programs for service members and employees who want to quit.
  • The local VA Vet Center.
  • In-person groups like Nicotine Anonymous. Find a local support group at www.nicotine-anonymous.org.
  • On-line support: www.Quitnet.com; www.Ashline.org; http://www.smokefree.gov
  • Local states: Your state may have a toll-free telephone quitline. Call the quitline for help.
