Alcohol & Drugs : Learn

Drinking In The Military

Heavy alcohol consumption is a significant problem in the military, affecting both uniformed service members and their families. Excessive alcohol use, either in the form of heavy drinking (drinking more than two drinks per day on average for men or more than one drink per day on average for women), or binge drinking (drinking 5 or more drinks during a single occasion for men or 4 or more drinks during a single occasion for women) can lead to an increased risk of numerous alcohol-related problems, including accidents, cancer and liver cirrhosis, as well as work, financial and social problems.

Drug Use In The Military

There has been a steep rise in the misuse of illegal and prescription drugs in the military. Prescription drugs account for the second most commonly abused category of drugs, behind marijuana. Prescription drug abuse is the use of a prescription medication in a way not intended by the prescribing healthcare provider. Misusing drugs can have severe consequences including a dishonorable discharge or jail time.

Prescription Drug Use In The Military

The prescription drugs most often prescribed to service members include painkillers or sedatives for anxiety and sleep disorders. Some service members abuse these prescription pain medications to cope or numb themselves from stress or painful feelings. Abusing drugs can lead to depression and addiction and in some instances if mixed with other drugs or alcohol can also lead to an overdose or death. Identifying prescription drug abuse early can help fix the problem before it becomes more serious or turns into an addiction.

Signs of prescription drug abuse include:
  • Stealing, forging or selling prescriptions
  • Taking higher doses than prescribed
  • Continually "losing" prescriptions, so more prescriptions must be written
  • Seeking prescriptions from more than one doctor
  • Excessive mood swings
  • Appearing to be high, unusually energetic or revved up, or sedated

How Do You Know When There Is a Problem?

If you think you may have a drug or alcohol problem, ask yourself:
  • Have you found that you were not able to stop drinking once you started?
  • Have you failed to do what was normally expected from you because of drinking?
  • Have you had a feeling of guilt or remorse after drinking or using drugs?
  • Do you need to have a first drink in the morning to get yourself going after a heavy drinking session?
  • Have you been unable to remember what happened the night before because you had been drinking?
  • Has a relative or friend been concerned about your drinking or suggested you cut down?
  • Have you been drinking more or using more drugs over time?
  • Have you tried unsuccessfully to cut down on alcohol or drugs?
  • Do you use alcohol or drugs in situations that are dangerous (i.e., driving or operating machinery)?
  • Do you drink or use drugs when you're alone, angry, sad or bored?
  • Do you use when reminded of upsetting deployment memories?
  • Do you ever use after telling yourself you won't?
If you answered "yes" to one or more of these questions you may want to consider decreasing your consumption with help from friends, family, and community groups (i.e., Alcoholics Anonymous or Narcotics Anonymous) or seek help from a professional.

Substance Misuse And Post-Traumatic Stress

Service members may use alcohol or drugs as a way to relieve post-traumatic stress, depression, irritability or upsetting memories. However, self-medicating with alcohol and/or drugs only provides temporary relief and can pose additional challenges, including:
  • Increased anger and irritability
  • Nervousness and paranoia
  • Reduced productivity at work
  • Increased risk for suicide, accidents or acts of violence
  • Conflicts with family members and friends
  • Difficulty concentrating, coping with trauma memories, or sleeping
  • Problems dealing with life stress
  • Feeling emotionally "numb"
  • Not wanting to be around people

Sleep Problems And Substance Abuse

Service members often find they have more nightmares or sleep terrors after a stressful or traumatic deployment. Traumatic means a life-threatening or terrifying event that produces intense fear, helplessness, or horror. These types of reactions are often associated with post-traumatic stress. To cope with nightmares and sleep terrors, some people use alcohol or drugs, or even avoid sleeping. Unfortunately, these methods not only worsen the sleep problem, they often lead to new problems.

Making Healthy Choices With Alcohol

It is important to know how much drinking is too much, or "when to say when." For most adults, consuming a moderate amount of alcohol is not harmful and will not lead to alcohol-related problems. Moderate alcohol use means:
  • Up to two drinks per day for men
  • Up to one drink per day for women
  • Up to one drink per day for older adults
One drink means:
  • A 12-ounce bottle of beer
  • A 5-ounce glass of wine
  • 1.5 ounces of 80 proof distilled liquor
NOTE: Moderate alcohol use is not safe for everyone. Abstain from drinking alcohol if:
  • You're pregnant
  • You have any health condition that could be affected by alcohol
  • You take medications that can interact with alcohol
  • You're a recovering alcoholic

Putting Moderate Drinking Into Action

To keep your drinking within moderate levels, you can try the following.
  • Make a plan:
  • Decide ahead of time how many drinks you will have.
  • Decide how you'll get home safely.
  • Commit to going home at a certain time.
  • Think of reasons to drink moderately (or not at all) before you go out, for example: "I want to wake up early" or "No thanks, I have the car keys."
  • Bring enough money for only a couple drinks and leave your credit cards at home.
  • Remember to eat before you drink. Having food in your body will affect how quickly or slowly alcohol enters your bloodstream.
  • Be fashionably late to the party or bar in order to limit your drinking time.
  • Order non-alcoholic drinks (water) between alcoholic drinks.
  • Know your limit. A good rule of thumb is one drink per hour.

Cut Back On Alcohol Without Giving It Up

Maybe it's time to speak honestly to yourself and cut back on your drinking. The tips here are designed for the social drinker who wants to cut back but doesn't need to stop drinking altogether. If you need more help, please see a health care provider.
  • Other than special occasions, limit your drinking to the level associated with optimal health. (Up to two drinks per day for men, one for women - and no stockpiling! Going without alcohol today doesn't increase the amount you can have tomorrow.)
  • Meet friends, dates, or business associates at a coffee bar, not a tavern.
  • Avoid drinking alone. If you learn to drink alone it makes it too easy to drink in excessive amounts. In addition, try limiting the amount of alcohol you keep at home.
  • Write down the reasons (family, social, legal, work, etc.) you are limiting your drinking (or drug use) on a piece of paper and and place it in your wallet to remind yourself daily.
  • Pick one or more days of the week to not drink at all.
  • Practice ways to say "no" when offered an alcoholic drink, for example "thank you but I'm planning on waking up early tomorrow morning to train for an upcoming race (or athletic event)." Say it quickly and firmly so that you don't give yourself time to change your mind.
  • Pace yourself by sipping your drink slowly and make sure you have no more than one drink an hour.
  • Take up a hobby, begin an exercise program, make new friends, or spend quality time with your family. Find something that you enjoy that will occupy the time during which you would usually be drinking.
  • Be assertive - don't be pressured into drinking.
  • Get help from family, friends, a healthcare provider, church member, or a self-help group like Alcoholics Anonymous (AA).
Don't give up. It may take several attempts to make healthy changes to your substance use habits.

Getting Home Safely

The following are tips on how to get home safely after drinking.
  • Designate a driver or plan ahead and order a taxi before departing the house.
  • Stow cash and taxi numbers in your wallet or purse.
  • Choose to take public transportation and research the operating schedules and fares before leaving the house.
  • If you walk home, make sure to use the buddy system (walk with someone) and stay in well-lit areas. Don't wander off alone, especially if you are drunk.
  • Get a hotel room or stay at a friend's house.

Abstinence: When Safe Use is No Use

Seriously consider quitting the use of drugs or alcohol altogether if:
  1. The substance you use controls you.
  2. You have chronic problems in relationships such as:
    1. Exposing loved ones to violence or harm
    2. Avoiding or distancing yourself emotionally from family
  3. Hurting family finances by spending too much money on alcohol or drugs. Some service members are motivated to quit when family members confront them about their drinking or drug use. If you use illegal drugs, STOP! You are risking your health, military career, relationships, and your legal record.

How To Get Help So You Can Quit:

Getting Help For Both Substance Use And Combat Stress/Post-Traumatic Stress (PTS)

Find a professional who works with substance abuse problems and PTS. Many VA hospitals offer support and treatment. Contact your local VA hospital or Vet Center for information about programs that work with substance use and combat stress problems. Your post-traumatic stress reactions may get worse when you initially quit drinking or using. Keep in mind that substance abuse can cause sleep and relationship problems, which make PTS worse. While challenging at first, working on both issues at the same time is more effective than working on them separately.
